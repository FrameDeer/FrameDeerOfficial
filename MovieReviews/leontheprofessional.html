Léon: The Professional (1994)

Genre: Action, Crime, Drama | Runtime: 110 min | Director: Luc Besson

Léon (Jean Reno) is a professional hitman living a solitary life in New York's Little Italy. His existence consists of cleaning jobs, milk drinking, caring for his plant, and occasional contract killings for his Italian employer Tony. His life changes dramatically when his 12-year-old neighbor Mathilda (Natalie Portman) returns home to discover her entire family has been murdered by corrupt DEA agent Norman Stansfield (Gary Oldman). Seeking refuge with Léon, Mathilda convinces him to teach her the skills of his trade so she can avenge her murdered younger brother. An unconventional relationship develops between the experienced hitman and the precocious girl as they navigate the dangerous world of organized crime, revenge, and unexpected emotional connection.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Luc Besson's masterpiece stands as one of the most unique and emotionally resonant action films ever made. Léon: The Professional transcends its genre trappings to become a profound exploration of loneliness, redemption, and the transformative power of human connection. The film balances brutal violence with surprising tenderness, creating a cinematic experience that remains unforgettable decades after its release.

The film's brilliance lies in the extraordinary chemistry between Jean Reno's Léon and Natalie Portman's Mathilda. Reno creates a character of remarkable depth and subtlety—a killer with childlike innocence, a professional who follows a strict code but yearns for basic human connection. His Léon is both terrifying and heartbreakingly vulnerable, a man whose emotional development was stunted by tragedy but who rediscovers his humanity through an unlikely relationship.

Natalie Portman's debut performance remains one of the most astonishing child actor performances in cinema history. At just 12 years old, she brings complexity, intelligence, and emotional depth to Mathilda that belies her age. The character's precociousness, trauma, and desperate need for connection create a fascinating dynamic with Léon's emotional isolation. Their relationship walks a delicate line between paternal bonding and uncomfortable romantic tension, creating layers of complexity that continue to spark discussion.

Gary Oldman delivers one of cinema's most memorable villain performances as Norman Stansfield. His portrayal of the corrupt, pill-popping, classical music-loving DEA agent is both terrifying and mesmerizing. Oldman finds the perfect balance between controlled menace and explosive madness, creating a villain who feels genuinely dangerous and unpredictable.

Besson's direction is masterful, creating a New York that feels both gritty and romanticized. The action sequences are choreographed with precision and style, but it's the quiet moments that truly define the film—Léon caring for his plant, Mathilda's "happy" game, their domestic routines. Eric Serra's score perfectly complements the film's emotional landscape, shifting seamlessly between tension, romance, and tragedy.

Final verdict: A cinematic tour de force that combines breathtaking action with profound emotional depth. Léon: The Professional remains Luc Besson's crowning achievement and a testament to the power of character-driven storytelling within genre filmmaking.

The Making of Léon: The Professional

Luc Besson wrote the screenplay as a personal project while feeling homesick during the production of "The Fifth Element." The character of Léon was originally conceived as a much older man, but when Jean Reno expressed interest, Besson rewrote the role specifically for him. The film was initially titled "The Cleaner" and went through several script revisions to balance the dark subject matter with emotional depth.

Casting Mathilda proved challenging, with over 2,000 young actresses auditioning for the role. Natalie Portman, then unknown, impressed Besson with her emotional maturity and intelligence during her audition. Her parents initially had concerns about the film's adult themes, but Besson worked closely with them to ensure Portman's comfort and safety during filming. The controversial aspects of Mathilda's character were carefully handled, with some of the more suggestive scenes being cut from the international release.

The production design created a distinctive visual style that contrasted Léon's sparse, minimalist apartment with the colorful chaos of New York City. The plant became a crucial symbolic element, representing Léon's need for something to care for and his own rootless existence. The action sequences were choreographed with a focus on realism rather than stylization, emphasizing Léon's methodical, professional approach to his work.

Gary Oldman's approach to Stansfield was largely improvisational, with Besson encouraging the actor to explore the character's manic energy. The famous "Mozart" scene was largely Oldman's creation, demonstrating his ability to find both terror and dark humor in villainy. The film's distinctive look was achieved through careful lighting and camera work that emphasized the emotional states of the characters.
