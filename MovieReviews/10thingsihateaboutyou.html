10 Things I Hate About You (1999)

Genre: Romance, Comedy, Teen | Runtime: 97 min | Director: Gil Junger

In this modern retelling of Shakespeare's "The Taming of the Shrew," new student Cameron (Joseph Gordon-Levitt) is smitten with Bianca (Larisa Oleynik), but she can't date until her anti-social older sister Kat (Julia Stiles) does. The problem is that Kat is an independent, sharp-tongued feminist who wants nothing to do with boys or dating. Enter Patrick Verona (Heath Ledger), the mysterious bad boy who's paid by Cameron's friend to woo Kat. As Patrick breaks through Kat's tough exterior, genuine feelings develop, leading to hilarious misunderstandings, heartfelt moments, and one of the most memorable prom scenes in teen movie history.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Gil Junger's directorial debut stands as one of the most intelligent and enduring teen comedies of the 1990s. What sets "10 Things I Hate About You" apart from its contemporaries is its clever Shakespearean foundation combined with genuinely witty dialogue and surprisingly complex character development.

The film's greatest strength lies in its subversion of typical teen movie tropes. Rather than presenting Kat as someone who needs to be "fixed," the film celebrates her intelligence and independence while exploring the vulnerability beneath her tough exterior. Julia Stiles delivers a breakthrough performance that balances sharp-tongued wit with genuine emotional depth.

Heath Ledger's performance as Patrick Verona announced the arrival of a major talent. His charismatic, layered portrayal transforms what could have been a stereotypical bad boy into a complex character with his own emotional baggage. The stadium steps serenade of "Can't Take My Eyes Off You" remains one of the most iconic romantic gestures in cinema history.

The supporting cast elevates the material significantly. Joseph Gordon-Levitt brings charm and sincerity to Cameron, while Larisa Oleynik perfectly captures Bianca's journey from superficial popularity to genuine self-awareness. David Krumholtz and Andrew Keegan provide excellent comic relief as the scheming friends.

The film's feminist undertones were remarkably progressive for 1999, with Kat's character serving as a positive representation of teenage feminism that neither preaches nor apologizes. Her poetry reading scene remains emotionally devastating and showcases Stiles' remarkable talent.

Final verdict: A near-perfect teen comedy that has aged beautifully, thanks to its smart writing, Shakespearean roots, and outstanding performances from a cast that would go on to become Hollywood A-listers.

The Making of 10 Things I Hate About You

The film began as a modern adaptation of Shakespeare's "The Taming of the Shrew" by first-time screenwriters Karen McCullah and Kirsten Smith. The writing duo intentionally created strong female characters who drove the plot, with Kat Stratford serving as a rare feminist protagonist in teen cinema. Their script attracted attention for its intelligent dialogue and clever Shakespearean parallels.

Casting proved crucial to the film's success. Heath Ledger, then relatively unknown in America, won the role of Patrick Verona after an impressive audition where he demonstrated both the character's bad-boy edge and unexpected sensitivity. Julia Stiles, who had Shakespearean theater experience, was perfect for the intellectually-minded Kat. The young cast bonded during the Seattle shoot, creating authentic chemistry on screen.

The iconic musical sequence where Patrick serenades Kat with "Can't Take My Eyes Off You" was Ledger's idea. Initially embarrassed by the scene, Ledger decided to fully commit to the over-the-top performance, dancing with the marching band and making grand gestures. His complete lack of self-consciousness turned what could have been cringe-worthy into one of the film's most beloved moments.

Stadium High School in Tacoma, Washington served as the primary filming location, with its castle-like architecture providing a storybook quality that complemented the Shakespearean roots. The production faced challenges with Seattle's unpredictable weather, but the overcast skies and green landscapes ultimately contributed to the film's distinctive Pacific Northwest aesthetic.
