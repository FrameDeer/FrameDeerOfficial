Central Intelligence (2016)

Genre: Action, Comedy | Runtime: 107 min | Director: Rawson Marshall Thurber

After he reconnects with an awkward pal from high school through Facebook, a mild-mannered accountant is lured into the world of international espionage. Calvin Joyner was a popular high school athlete who has grown up to be an accountant. He's bored with his life until he's contacted online by Bob Stone, who was an overweight, bullied outcast in high school. Now Bob is a ripped CIA agent who needs Calvin's accounting skills to help him stop a terrorist plot. Calvin is quickly swept up in a dangerous world of shoot-outs, double-crosses, and espionage that could get them both killed in more ways than he can count.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Dwayne Johnson and Kevin Hart's unlikely buddy comedy delivers exactly what you'd expect: solid laughs, surprising chemistry, and plenty of action. Central Intelligence succeeds primarily because of the fantastic pairing of its two leads, who play off each other's strengths perfectly.

What makes this film work better than many other action-comedies is the reversal of traditional roles. Johnson plays the socially awkward but physically imposing Bob Stone, while Hart portrays the straight-laced, "normal" accountant caught in an extraordinary situation. This dynamic creates fresh comedic opportunities that both actors exploit to great effect.

The film's heart comes from its exploration of high school trauma and personal transformation. Johnson's character, despite his incredible physical transformation, still carries the emotional scars of being bullied. This adds an unexpected layer of depth to what could have been a straightforward comedy.

The action sequences are well-executed and surprisingly inventive, particularly the hotel fight scene that showcases Johnson's physical comedy chops. The plot, while predictable, serves as a competent framework for the character interactions and comedic set pieces.

Where the film occasionally stumbles is in its pacing and some underdeveloped supporting characters. However, the central relationship between Johnson and Hart is so entertaining that these flaws are easily overlooked.

Final verdict: A fun, entertaining buddy comedy that makes the most of its star power. While not groundbreaking, it delivers solid laughs and proves Johnson and Hart are one of comedy's most effective pairings.

The Making of Central Intelligence

The concept for Central Intelligence was specifically developed to capitalize on the natural chemistry between Dwayne Johnson and Kevin Hart. Director Rawson Marshall Thurber wrote the script with both actors in mind, creating characters that would highlight their comedic strengths while allowing for unexpected character depth.

One of the film's most memorable elements is Johnson's transformation into the awkward, fanny-pack-wearing Bob Stone. Johnson embraced the character's vulnerability, drawing on his own experiences with insecurity during his youth. The famous unicorn t-shirt became an instant pop culture icon and was Johnson's idea, wanting the character to have a signature item that represented his quirky personality.

The high school flashback scenes required extensive prosthetic work to create Johnson's overweight teenage version. The production used both practical effects and digital enhancement to achieve the convincing transformation. Interestingly, Kevin Hart also had to wear padding to appear more muscular in these flashbacks, as his character was the high school athlete.

Much of the improvisation between Johnson and Hart made it into the final cut, with the director encouraging their natural banter. The hotel fight scene, a highlight of the film, was carefully choreographed to blend action and comedy, with Johnson performing many of his own stunts while maintaining the character's awkward physicality.
