Mission: Impossible (1996)

Genre: Action, Adventure, Thriller | Runtime: 110 min | Director: Brian De Palma

An American agent, under false suspicion of disloyalty, must discover and expose the real spy without the help of his organization. When IMF agent Ethan Hunt (Tom Cruise) is framed for the murders of his entire team during a mission in Prague, he becomes a fugitive hunted by his own agency. With the help of two disavowed agents, Hunt races against time to uncover the real traitor and clear his name. The mission takes him from the CIA's most secure vault in Langley to a high-speed chase through the Channel Tunnel, testing his skills and loyalty at every turn.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Brian De Palma's stylish direction launched one of cinema's most enduring action franchises. Mission: Impossible successfully translates the classic TV series' espionage intrigue into a sleek, modern thriller that established Tom Cruise as an action superstar.

The film's greatest strength lies in its masterful set pieces, particularly the iconic CIA vault break-in sequence. Shot with breathtaking tension and minimal dialogue, this scene has become one of the most memorable moments in action cinema history, showcasing De Palma's Hitchcockian sensibilities.

Tom Cruise delivers a career-defining performance as Ethan Hunt, blending physical prowess with emotional vulnerability. His commitment to performing his own stunts, including the famous wire-suspended vault scene, set the standard for the franchise's signature practical effects approach.

The complex plot twists and double-crosses keep audiences guessing throughout, with De Palma expertly balancing espionage intrigue with explosive action. The film's opening sequence in Prague remains a masterclass in building tension and establishing character dynamics.

What makes Mission: Impossible truly special is how it respects its television origins while expanding the scope for the big screen. Lalo Schifrin's iconic theme music is brilliantly integrated, and the self-destructing message concept adds a layer of classic spy thriller charm.

Final verdict: A stylish, intelligent action thriller that launched a legendary franchise. Essential viewing for spy genre enthusiasts and anyone who appreciates masterfully crafted suspense sequences.

The Making of Mission: Impossible

Brian De Palma brought his signature style to the adaptation of the classic television series. The director, known for his work on Scarface and The Untouchables, approached the material with a focus on suspense and visual storytelling rather than pure action.

The film's most iconic sequence—the break-in at CIA headquarters—was a marvel of practical effects and tension-building. Tom Cruise insisted on performing the wire-suspended vault scene himself, spending weeks training to move silently while suspended from wires. The scene was shot in a massive set built at Pinewood Studios, with Cruise actually hanging 30 feet above the ground.

The famous opening sequence in Prague was heavily influenced by De Palma's admiration for Hitchcock. The intricate cross-cutting between multiple characters and the gradual building of tension established the film's sophisticated espionage tone from the very beginning.

Production faced significant challenges, including rewriting the script during filming. The original screenplay went through multiple revisions, with several key scenes being developed on set. The train tunnel finale was particularly complex, requiring coordination between British and French railway authorities.

De Palma's decision to kill off most of the IMF team in the opening act was controversial but proved crucial in establishing the film's high stakes and Ethan Hunt's isolation. This bold narrative choice set the franchise apart from its television origins and created immediate audience investment in Hunt's quest for redemption.
