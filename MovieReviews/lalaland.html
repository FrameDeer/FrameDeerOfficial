La La Land (2016)

Genre: Musical, Romance, Drama | Runtime: 128 min | Director: Damien Chazelle

In modern-day Los Angeles, aspiring actress Mia (Emma Stone) serves coffee between auditions while dreaming of Hollywood stardom, and dedicated jazz pianist Sebastian (Ryan Gosling) plays in dingy bars while hoping to open his own club. Their paths cross in traffic, at parties, and through chance encounters, eventually blossoming into a passionate romance as they pursue their artistic dreams in a city that crushes as many aspirations as it fulfills. Through four seasons, their relationship unfolds against the backdrop of LA's magical landscapes, from the Hollywood Hills to the Griffith Observatory, as they navigate the tension between personal ambition and romantic love. The film celebrates and critiques the dreamers who flock to Los Angeles while paying homage to classic Hollywood musicals through breathtaking song-and-dance numbers and a poignant exploration of what we sacrifice for our dreams.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Damien Chazelle creates a modern cinematic masterpiece that revitalizes the Hollywood musical for the 21st century while telling a profoundly moving story about love, ambition, and the choices that define our lives. La La Land is that rare film that works simultaneously as glorious entertainment, technical marvel, and deep emotional experience, reminding audiences why we fell in love with movies in the first place.

The film's opening number, "Another Day of Sun," immediately announces its ambitious scope—a spectacular single-take musical sequence on a Los Angeles freeway that sets the tone for what follows. This breathtaking introduction establishes the film's central theme: finding magic in the mundane, pursuing dreams in a city of both endless possibility and crushing reality. Chazelle's direction throughout is both nostalgic and innovative, paying homage to classics like Singin' in the Rain and The Umbrellas of Cherbourg while creating something entirely fresh.

Emma Stone delivers an Oscar-winning performance of astonishing depth and vulnerability as Mia. Her rendition of "Audition (The Fools Who Dream)" is not just a show-stopping musical moment but the emotional heart of the film—a raw, honest expression of what it means to be an artist risking everything for your passion. Stone captures both the wide-eyed optimism and weary resilience of someone chasing a dream in Hollywood.

Ryan Gosling brings charm, wit, and surprising musical talent to Sebastian, creating a character who is both romantic idealist and pragmatic survivor. His chemistry with Stone—honed through two previous films together—feels effortless and authentic, making their romance both swoon-worthy and believable. Gosling's dedication to learning piano for the role pays off in spades, particularly in the beautiful "City of Stars" sequences.

The technical achievements are staggering: Linus Sandgren's cinematography paints Los Angeles in magical, saturated colors; Justin Hurwitz's score and songs are instant classics that serve character and story; and the production design creates a Los Angeles that is both recognizably real and wonderfully heightened. The film's final montage—a heartbreaking "what if" sequence—stands as one of the most emotionally powerful endings in recent cinema.

Final verdict: A breathtaking achievement that reminds us of cinema's power to transport, move, and inspire. La La Land is more than a musical—it's a love letter to dreamers, artists, and anyone who has ever had to choose between love and ambition, wrapped in one of the most beautifully crafted films of the 21st century.

The Making of La La Land

La La Land represents the culmination of Damien Chazelle's long-standing passion for musicals and his desire to create a modern film that captured the magic of classic Hollywood while telling a contemporary story. The project had been in development since 2010, with Chazelle initially conceiving it as a low-budget film before Whiplash's success allowed him to realize his ambitious vision.

The casting of Emma Stone and Ryan Gosling marked their third collaboration, building on the chemistry they developed in Crazy, Stupid, Love and Gangster Squad. Both actors underwent extensive training for their roles—Stone worked with a vocal coach for months to prepare her singing, while Gosling learned piano, practicing for hours daily to convincingly portray a jazz pianist. Their dedication to performing the musical numbers themselves, rather than using doubles or extensive editing, gives the film an authentic, lived-in quality.

The film's musical numbers were choreographed by Mandy Moore (not the singer/actress) and required meticulous planning and rehearsal. The opening freeway sequence, "Another Day of Sun," was shot during two days of 100-degree heat on a closed freeway ramp with 30 professional dancers and 100 extras. The famous Griffith Observatory planetarium sequence used practical effects and careful choreography to create the illusion of weightlessness and magic.

Justin Hurwitz's score and songs were developed over several years, with the composer working closely with Chazelle to ensure the music served both the emotional story and the film's nostalgic tone. The songs were recorded live on set rather than in post-production, adding to the film's authentic, spontaneous feel.

The production faced the challenge of making Los Angeles feel both realistic and magical. Cinematographer Linus Sandgren used CinemaScope lenses and vibrant color palettes to create a heightened reality that paid homage to Technicolor musicals while feeling contemporary. The film's visual language—from the golden hour lighting to the carefully composed widescreen frames—became instantly iconic.
