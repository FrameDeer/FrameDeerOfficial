The Apprentice (2024)

Genre: Biography, Drama, History | Runtime: 120 min | Director: Ali Abbasi

The film chronicles the formative years of Donald Trump's career in 1970s and 1980s New York, focusing on his complex relationship with political fixer Roy Cohn. Sebastian Stan stars as the young Trump, portraying his transformation from an ambitious real estate heir to a formidable business figure under Cohn's ruthless tutelage. Jeremy Strong delivers a powerful performance as Roy Cohn, the infamous lawyer who teaches Trump his cutthroat approach to business and politics. The film explores how Cohn's philosophy of "always attack, never apologize" and his mastery of media manipulation shaped Trump's worldview and business tactics. Through their partnership, we witness the emergence of the tactics and persona that would later define one of the most controversial figures in modern American history.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Ali Abbasi's provocative biographical drama offers a compelling and nuanced look at the making of a modern political phenomenon. Rather than focusing on Trump's presidency, the film smartly examines his formative years, providing crucial context for understanding how his particular brand of business and media savvy developed. This approach makes for a more insightful character study than a straightforward political commentary.

Sebastian Stan delivers a transformative performance that goes beyond mere impersonation. He captures the young Trump's ambition, insecurity, and growing ruthlessness with remarkable subtlety. Stan's portrayal shows us the man behind the persona—a real estate heir desperate to prove himself to his father and New York's elite establishment. His physical transformation is impressive, but it's the psychological depth he brings to the role that makes the performance truly memorable.

Jeremy Strong is mesmerizing as Roy Cohn, creating one of the year's most compelling supporting performances. He embodies Cohn's Machiavellian brilliance and personal demons with equal intensity, showing us the man who would become Trump's most influential mentor. The chemistry between Stan and Strong drives the film, with their scenes together crackling with tension and psychological complexity.

The film's production design perfectly captures the gaudy opulence of 1970s and 80s New York real estate culture. From Trump's early office spaces to the development of Trump Tower, the visual storytelling reinforces the themes of ambition and image-crafting that define the protagonist's journey. The attention to period detail helps ground the story in its specific historical moment.

What makes The Apprentice particularly effective is its refusal to descend into caricature or heavy-handed political messaging. Instead, it presents a clear-eyed examination of how power, media manipulation, and business tactics evolved during this crucial period. The film raises important questions about mentorship, ambition, and the American Dream without providing easy answers.

Final verdict: A brilliantly acted, thoughtfully crafted biographical drama that provides essential context for understanding one of the most influential figures of our time. Whether you're politically engaged or simply appreciate great filmmaking, The Apprentice offers a compelling character study that transcends partisan politics.

The Making of The Apprentice

The development process was extensive given the sensitive nature of the subject matter. Director Ali Abbasi and screenwriter Gabriel Sherman spent years researching Trump's early career, drawing from historical records, interviews, and previously unpublished materials. Their approach focused on creating a character study rather than a political polemic, aiming to understand rather than judge their controversial subject.

Sebastian Stan underwent a remarkable transformation for the role, working with dialect coaches to capture Trump's distinctive speech patterns and mannerisms. However, Stan's approach went deeper than surface imitation—he studied Trump's psychological profile, business decisions, and personal relationships to understand the man behind the public persona. The physical transformation included extensive prosthetics and makeup work to accurately portray Trump across different ages.

Jeremy Strong's preparation for playing Roy Cohn was equally intensive. Known for his method approach, Strong immersed himself in Cohn's history, studying everything from his legal tactics to his personal life and eventual illness. Strong worked closely with the director to explore the complex mentor-protégé relationship that forms the emotional core of the film.

The production faced unique challenges in recreating 1970s and 1980s New York. While some scenes were shot in New York, much of the production took place in Montreal, where period-appropriate locations were more readily available. The art department conducted extensive research to accurately recreate Trump's early office spaces and the development of iconic properties like Trump Tower.
