The Great Gatsby (2013)

Genre: Drama, Romance | Runtime: 143 min | Director: Baz Luhrmann

Midwest native Nick Carraway arrives in 1922 New York in search of the American dream. Nick, a would-be writer, moves in next-door to millionaire Jay Gatsby and across the bay from his cousin Daisy and her philandering husband, Tom. Thus, Nick becomes drawn into the captivating world of the wealthy and — as he bears witness to their illusions and deceits — pens a tale of impossible love, incorruptible dreams, and tragedy. Amidst the decadent parties and lavish lifestyle of the Jazz Age, Gatsby's singular obsession with recapturing the past leads him on a collision course with destiny.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Baz Luhrmann's extravagant adaptation of F. Scott Fitzgerald's classic novel brings the Jazz Age to life with breathtaking visual splendor and contemporary energy. While divisive among critics for its modern stylistic choices, this version of The Great Gatsby captures the essence of Fitzgerald's themes about the American Dream, class, and the corruption of innocence in a way that resonates with modern audiences.

The film's most striking achievement is its visual and auditory landscape. Luhrmann creates a world of opulent excess that both dazzles and repels, perfectly mirroring the novel's critique of wealth and social climbing. The anachronistic soundtrack, featuring artists like Jay-Z, Lana Del Rey, and Florence + The Machine, surprisingly enhances rather than detracts from the period setting, emphasizing the timeless nature of the story's themes.

Leonardo DiCaprio delivers a masterful performance as Jay Gatsby, perfectly capturing the character's enigmatic charm, desperate optimism, and tragic vulnerability. His introduction—turning to face the camera with a champagne glass in hand as fireworks explode behind him—is one of the most iconic character entrances in recent cinema. DiCaprio embodies Gatsby's romantic idealism and the profound sadness that lies beneath his glittering facade.

Carey Mulligan brings depth and complexity to Daisy Buchanan, portraying her not as a simple villain but as a product of her time and circumstances—a woman trapped by societal expectations and her own weakness. The chemistry between DiCaprio and Mulligan creates palpable tension and longing, making their doomed romance both beautiful and heartbreaking.

While the film takes creative liberties with Fitzgerald's text, it remains remarkably faithful to the novel's spirit and central themes. The use of 3D technology, often criticized as a gimmick, actually serves the story by immersing viewers in Gatsby's overwhelming world of illusion and excess.

Final verdict: A visually stunning and emotionally resonant adaptation that may divide purists but successfully translates Fitzgerald's classic to the modern cinematic language. DiCaprio's Gatsby alone makes this essential viewing.

The Making of The Great Gatsby

Baz Luhrmann's vision for Gatsby was years in development, with the director wanting to create an adaptation that would speak to contemporary audiences while honoring Fitzgerald's text. Luhrmann worked closely with his wife and creative partner Catherine Martin to design a visual world that would capture both the opulence and the emptiness of the Jazz Age.

The production built massive sets in Sydney, Australia, including a full-scale replica of Gatsby's mansion and the elaborate party sequences that required hundreds of extras. The famous Art Deco-inspired production design combined historical accuracy with Luhrmann's signature theatrical flair, creating a world that felt both authentic and dreamlike.

Leonardo DiCaprio's preparation for the role involved extensive research into the character's psychology and the historical context of the 1920s. DiCaprio worked with dialect coaches to perfect Gatsby's affected upper-class accent, which subtly reveals his working-class origins. The actor's understanding of Gatsby's tragic optimism and self-invention made his performance particularly nuanced.

The soundtrack became a major creative element, with Luhrmann collaborating with Jay-Z to executive produce a score that blended 1920s jazz with contemporary hip-hop and pop. This controversial choice was intended to make the film's themes of excess, aspiration, and social climbing feel immediate and relevant to modern viewers.

The 3D cinematography was used not just for spectacle but to enhance the storytelling, particularly in the party scenes where the depth of field creates a sense of overwhelming sensory experience. Luhrmann wanted viewers to feel like they were actually attending Gatsby's legendary parties.
