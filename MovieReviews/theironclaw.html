The Iron Claw (2023)

Genre: Drama, Sports, Biography | Runtime: 132 min | Director: Sean Durkin

The true story of the Von Erich family, a dynasty of professional wrestlers who made a huge impact on the sport in the 1980s. Patriarch Fritz Von Erich (Holt McCallany) pushes his sons—Kevin (Zac Efron), Kerry (Jeremy Allen White), David (Harris Dickinson), and Mike (Stanley Simons)—to become champions, driven by his own unfulfilled wrestling ambitions. As the brothers rise to fame in the competitive world of professional wrestling, they must navigate the intense pressure from their father, the physical demands of the sport, and a family curse that seems to haunt them. Through triumph and tragedy, the Von Erich brothers support each other while wrestling with their father's expectations and the dark shadow that follows their family name.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Sean Durkin's powerful family tragedy transcends the sports genre to become one of the most emotionally devastating and beautifully crafted films of recent years. The Iron Claw is less about wrestling and more about the bonds of brotherhood, the weight of legacy, and the tragic consequences of impossible expectations.

Zac Efron delivers a career-defining performance as Kevin Von Erich, showcasing dramatic depth and physical transformation that completely shatters his teen idol image. His portrayal of the protective older brother, trying to hold his family together amid unimaginable tragedy, is both powerful and heartbreakingly vulnerable. The physicality he brings to the role—both in the wrestling sequences and in embodying Kevin's quiet strength—is nothing short of remarkable.

The brotherly chemistry among the cast feels authentic and deeply moving. Jeremy Allen White brings intensity and tragic ambition to Kerry, Harris Dickinson captures David's charismatic showmanship, and Stanley Simons portrays Mike's artistic sensitivity with heartbreaking delicacy. Their interactions create a believable family dynamic that makes the unfolding tragedies all the more devastating.

Holt McCallany is terrifyingly effective as Fritz Von Erich, the patriarch whose relentless drive and outdated notions of masculinity ultimately contribute to his family's downfall. McCallany avoids caricature, instead creating a complex portrait of a man who genuinely believes he's preparing his sons for success, unaware of the damage he's causing.

Final verdict: A masterpiece of American tragedy that balances brutal physicality with profound emotional depth. The Iron Claw will leave you emotionally wrecked but deeply moved—a testament to the enduring power of brotherly love in the face of unimaginable loss.

The Making of The Iron Claw

Writer-director Sean Durkin spent years developing the project, wanting to tell the Von Erich story with the sensitivity and depth it deserved. Durkin conducted extensive research, including meeting with Kevin Von Erich and other family members to ensure the portrayal was accurate and respectful. The script went through multiple revisions to balance the wrestling action with the family drama, focusing on the emotional core of the story rather than sensationalizing the tragedies.

The cast underwent intensive wrestling training to perform their own stunts and make the wrestling sequences authentic. Zac Efron trained for months with professional wrestlers, learning the specific moves and style of 1980s wrestling. The actors also studied footage of the real Von Erich brothers to capture their mannerisms and wrestling techniques. This dedication to authenticity extended to the period-accurate wrestling rings, costumes, and camera work that replicated the look of 1980s wrestling broadcasts.

Physical transformations were crucial for the cast to embody the wrestlers. Zac Efron underwent a significant body transformation, gaining over 20 pounds of muscle for the role. The actors also had to learn the specific physicality of each brother—from Kevin's powerful athleticism to Kerry's technical precision and David's charismatic showmanship.

The film's visual approach was carefully designed to distinguish between the glamorous public image of the Von Erichs and their private struggles. Cinematographer Mátyás Erdély used different visual styles for the wrestling sequences (bright, saturated colors reminiscent of 1980s television) and the family scenes (more natural, intimate lighting) to create contrast between their public and private lives.
