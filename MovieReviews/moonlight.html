Moonlight (2016)

Genre: Drama, LGBTQ+ | Runtime: 111 min | Director: Barry Jenkins

A timeless story of human connection and self-discovery, Moonlight chronicles the life of Chiron, a young Black man from Miami, as he navigates his childhood, adolescence, and adulthood. Divided into three chapters that explore identity, sexuality, and the struggle to become oneself, the film follows Chiron's journey as he grapples with his drug-addicted mother, school bullies, and his own emerging sexuality. Through his encounters with Juan, a compassionate drug dealer who becomes a father figure, and Kevin, his childhood friend and first love, Chiron discovers the painful beauty of growing up and the haunting power of the people who shape our lives.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Barry Jenkins' masterpiece is a poetic and deeply moving exploration of Black masculinity, queer identity, and the universal search for self. Moonlight transcends its specific cultural context to become a profoundly human story about the people who see us for who we truly are.

The film's triptych structure—showing Chiron as a child (Little), teenager (Chiron), and adult (Black)—creates a powerful narrative arc that captures how childhood experiences shape our adult selves. Each chapter feels like a complete short film while contributing to the overarching emotional journey.

Mahershala Ali's performance as Juan, though brief in screen time, leaves an indelible mark on the film and the viewer. His portrayal of a drug dealer with profound paternal instincts creates one of cinema's most complex and compassionate characters. The beach scene where he teaches Little to float serves as the film's emotional and metaphorical centerpiece.

The three actors portraying Chiron—Alex Hibbert, Ashton Sanders, and Trevante Rhodes—deliver remarkably cohesive performances that capture the same soul at different life stages. Their collective work creates a character study of rare depth and authenticity.

James Laxton's cinematography uses Miami's vibrant colors and textures to create a visual language that feels both realistic and dreamlike. The intimate close-ups and fluid camera movements immerse us in Chiron's subjective experience, while Nicholas Britell's haunting, classical-inspired score elevates the emotional landscape.

Final verdict: A landmark achievement in American cinema that redefines what coming-of-age stories can be. Moonlight is a tender, heartbreaking, and ultimately hopeful portrait of a human being learning to see himself in a world that refuses to.

The Making of Moonlight

Barry Jenkins adapted the screenplay from Tarell Alvin McCraney's unpublished semi-autobiographical play "In Moonlight Black Boys Look Blue." Both Jenkins and McCraney grew up in Liberty City, Miami, and had mothers who struggled with drug addiction, lending the story profound personal authenticity. This shared experience created one of the most authentic portrayals of Miami's African American community in cinema history.

The film was shot in just 25 days on a modest $1.5 million budget. Jenkins employed an intimate, collaborative approach with his cast, often rehearsing scenes extensively and encouraging improvisation. The three actors playing Chiron at different ages never met during production to maintain the purity of each performance.

James Laxton's cinematography used the Arri Alexa camera with special lenses to create a heightened, almost dreamlike quality while maintaining naturalism. The distinctive color palette—dominated by blues, purples, and Miami's vibrant tones—became a character in itself, reflecting Chiron's emotional journey and the film's poetic realism.

Nicholas Britell's score incorporated "chopped and screwed" techniques, slowing down classical-inspired compositions to create a haunting, atmospheric sound that mirrored Chiron's internal state. The music became an essential element in conveying the unspoken emotions and memories that shape the protagonist's life.
