Ad Astra (2019)

Genre: Sci-Fi, Drama, Adventure | Runtime: 123 min | Director: James Gray

Astronaut Roy McBride (Brad Pitt) travels to the outer edges of the solar system to find his missing father (Tommy Lee Jones) and unravel a mystery that threatens the survival of our planet. His journey will uncover secrets that challenge the nature of human existence and our place in the cosmos. As Roy ventures deeper into space, he must confront his own emotional void and the legacy of a father who chose cosmic exploration over family. The film presents a near-future where space travel has become commercialized but the human heart remains the final frontier.

FrameDeer Thoughts

James Gray's contemplative space epic stands as one of the most visually stunning and philosophically ambitious science fiction films of recent years. Ad Astra combines the grandeur of interstellar adventure with the intimacy of psychological drama, creating a unique cinematic experience that explores the vastness of space alongside the depths of human emotion.

Brad Pitt delivers a masterful internal performance as Roy McBride, an astronaut so emotionally controlled that his heart rate never rises above 80 bpm, even in life-threatening situations. Pitt's portrayal of repressed emotion and quiet desperation is a tour de force of subtle acting, conveying volumes through stillness and minimal dialogue. His journey becomes a metaphor for the human condition—searching for meaning in the infinite while grappling with earthly connections.

The film's scientific realism sets it apart from typical space operas. From the accurate depiction of orbital mechanics to the haunting silence of space, Ad Astra grounds its extraordinary premise in plausible science. The action sequences, particularly the lunar rover chase and the zero-gravity confrontation, are thrilling while maintaining the film's overall contemplative tone. The visual effects seamlessly blend practical and digital elements to create a believable near-future spacefaring civilization.

Hoyte van Hoytema's cinematography is nothing short of breathtaking, capturing both the sublime beauty and terrifying emptiness of space. The use of practical lighting and careful composition creates a visual language that feels both epic and intimate. Max Richter's haunting score perfectly complements the visuals, blending electronic and orchestral elements to evoke the film's themes of isolation and connection.

Final verdict: A meditative and visually spectacular journey that combines hard science fiction with profound emotional depth. Ad Astra may not satisfy viewers seeking conventional action, but for those willing to engage with its philosophical questions and psychological complexity, it offers a rich and rewarding experience that lingers long after the credits roll.

The Making of Ad Astra

James Gray spent nearly a decade developing Ad Astra, originally conceiving it as an "Apocalypse Now in space." The director conducted extensive research with NASA scientists to ensure scientific accuracy while maintaining the film's poetic and philosophical ambitions. Gray worked closely with production designer Kevin Thompson to create a near-future vision of space exploration that felt both technologically advanced and commercially plausible.

Brad Pitt's involvement was crucial to getting the film made, with the actor also serving as producer through his company Plan B Entertainment. Pitt underwent astronaut training and worked with psychologists to understand the psychological profile of someone who remains calm under extreme pressure. His character's emotional repression required a performance built on subtlety and internal conflict rather than overt expression.

The film's visual effects combined practical and digital techniques to achieve its stunning realism. Cinematographer Hoyte van Hoytema (Interstellar, Dunkirk) used innovative camera rigs and lighting techniques to simulate zero-gravity environments. Many of the spacecraft interiors were built as practical sets that could rotate and move, giving the actors authentic physical experiences to react to. The lunar surface was created using giant sets with practical lighting effects.

Gray's approach to the screenplay emphasized psychological realism over conventional plot mechanics. The director described the film as "the most expensive art film ever made," focusing on character development and thematic depth rather than traditional narrative structure. The extensive voiceover narration, delivered by Pitt in a flat, emotionless tone, was a deliberate creative choice to emphasize the character's emotional isolation.
