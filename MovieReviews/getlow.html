Get Low (2009)

Genre: Drama, Mystery | Runtime: 103 min | Director: Aaron Schneider

In 1930s Tennessee, Felix Bush (Robert Duvall), a mysterious and reclusive hermit who has lived alone in the woods for 40 years, suddenly emerges with an unusual request: he wants to throw his own funeral party while he's still alive to hear what people have to say about him. He enlists the help of Frank Quinn (Bill Murray), a cynical funeral home owner looking for financial salvation, and Quinn's earnest young assistant Buddy Robinson (Lucas Black). As they organize the "living funeral," Felix's true motives begin to surface, revealing a decades-old secret involving lost love, guilt, and redemption. The event attracts widespread attention, forcing Felix to confront his past and the tragic mystery that drove him into isolation years earlier.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Aaron Schneider makes an impressive feature directorial debut with this beautifully crafted character study that explores themes of guilt, redemption, and the weight of secrets. Get Low is a quiet, contemplative film that proves sometimes the most powerful stories are told in whispers rather than shouts, anchored by two of the finest actors of their generation delivering career-highlight performances.

The film's strength lies in its patient storytelling and deep character development. Rather than rushing to reveal Felix Bush's mystery, the screenplay carefully builds anticipation while developing rich, believable characters. The 1930s Southern setting feels authentic and lived-in, creating a perfect backdrop for this tale of rural America and the secrets that can fester in small communities.

Robert Duvall delivers a masterclass in subtle acting as Felix Bush. He creates a character who is simultaneously intimidating and vulnerable, mysterious yet deeply human. Duvall's ability to convey decades of pain and regret through subtle gestures and expressions is nothing short of remarkable. His final monologue, where he reveals the truth about his past, stands as one of the most powerful scenes in modern cinema.

Bill Murray provides perfect counterpoint as Frank Quinn, bringing his signature dry wit and world-weariness to the role of a funeral director who sees Felix's unusual request as both a business opportunity and a curiosity. The chemistry between Duvall and Murray is electric, with Murray's cynical humor playing beautifully against Duvall's gruff intensity. Their scenes together are a masterclass in acting partnership.

Sissy Spacek adds warmth and depth as Mattie Darrow, a woman from Felix's past who represents the life he might have lived. Lucas Black holds his own against the veteran cast as Buddy, the moral center who begins to understand that there's more to Felix's story than just an old man's eccentricity.

Final verdict: A beautifully understated film that proves the most compelling mysteries are those of the human heart. Get Low is a testament to the power of great acting and thoughtful storytelling, reminding us that redemption is always possible, no matter how long we've been carrying our burdens.

The Making of Get Low

Get Low was inspired by the true story of Felix "Bush" Breazeale, a Tennessee hermit who actually did throw his own funeral party in 1938. The screenplay by Chris Provenzano and C. Gaby Mitchell spent years in development, with the project attracting interest due to its unique premise and strong central character. First-time feature director Aaron Schneider, an Oscar-winning cinematographer, was drawn to the project for its character-driven storytelling and Southern Gothic atmosphere.

Robert Duvall was attached to the project early on, recognizing the depth and complexity of the Felix Bush character. He spent time researching hermits and reclusive individuals to understand the psychology of someone who would choose isolation. Duvall also worked on developing Felix's distinctive physicality and speech patterns to create a character who felt authentic to the 1930s Appalachian setting.

Bill Murray's casting came about through his interest in working with Duvall and his appreciation for the script's unique blend of humor and pathos. Murray brought his improvisational skills to the role, adding layers of dry wit to Frank Quinn that weren't necessarily in the original script. The dynamic between Duvall's intense dramatic performance and Murray's comedic timing created the film's unique tone.

The production faced challenges in recreating 1930s rural Tennessee on a modest budget. The filmmakers shot in Georgia, taking advantage of the state's tax incentives and period-appropriate locations. Attention to historical detail was crucial, from the authentic funeral home set to the vintage automobiles and costumes that helped establish the Depression-era setting.

The film's climax—Felix's confession at his own funeral—required careful direction and preparation. Duvall worked extensively on the monologue, understanding that this moment needed to pay off the mystery that had been building throughout the film. The result is one of the most powerful and emotionally raw scenes in either actor's career.
