Don't Worry Darling (2022)

Genre: Thriller, Drama, Mystery | Runtime: 123 min | Director: Olivia Wilde

Alice (Florence Pugh) and Jack (Harry Styles) are lucky to be living in the idealized community of Victory, an experimental 1950s company town where the men who work for the top-secret Victory Project are rewarded with comfortable homes for their families. While the husbands spend their days working on the "development of progressive materials," their wives enjoy the beauty, luxury and debauchery of their seemingly perfect paradise. However, when cracks in her idyllic life begin to appear, Alice can't help but question exactly what they're doing in Victory, and why. As she investigates the disturbing truths behind the Victory Project, Alice discovers that the price of perfection is far more dangerous than she ever imagined.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Olivia Wilde's ambitious sophomore directorial effort delivers stunning visuals and a compelling central performance, even as it struggles to fully deliver on its intriguing premise. Don't Worry Darling is a film of remarkable style and atmosphere that captures the aesthetic perfection of 1950s suburbia while slowly revealing the sinister underpinnings beneath the surface. The film's production design and cinematography are nothing short of breathtaking, creating a world that feels both idyllic and unnervingly artificial.

The film's greatest strength is Florence Pugh's powerhouse performance as Alice. Pugh carries the entire film on her shoulders, delivering a nuanced portrayal of a woman slowly unraveling as she discovers the dark truths of her perfect life. Her ability to convey mounting terror, confusion, and determination without ever losing the character's emotional core is remarkable. Pugh's performance elevates the material and provides the emotional anchor that keeps audiences invested through the film's uneven narrative.

Chris Pine delivers a chilling and charismatic performance as Frank, the enigmatic leader of the Victory Project. Pine brings just the right amount of charm and menace to the role, creating a villain who is both seductive and terrifying. His scenes with Pugh are among the film's strongest, particularly their tense dinner party confrontation that crackles with psychological tension.

Where the film struggles is in its execution of the mystery and eventual reveal. While the premise is compelling and the atmosphere effectively unsettling, the final act feels rushed and somewhat underwhelming given the meticulous buildup. The social commentary about gender roles and patriarchal control, while relevant, is delivered with less subtlety than the film's visual storytelling. Harry Styles, while bringing undeniable charisma to his role, sometimes struggles to match the intensity of his co-stars in the more dramatic moments.

Final verdict: A visually stunning thriller with a phenomenal central performance from Florence Pugh that's ultimately let down by an underdeveloped script and uneven execution. While it doesn't fully deliver on its promising premise, it remains an ambitious and often captivating cinematic experience.

The Making of Don't Worry Darling

The production was surrounded by significant controversy and media attention, often overshadowing the film itself. From reported tensions between director Olivia Wilde and star Florence Pugh to the very public relationship between Wilde and Harry Styles, the film became a tabloid fixture even before its release. This intense scrutiny created additional pressure on a production already challenged by COVID-19 protocols and the difficulties of creating a period piece on a moderate budget.

The film's stunning visual aesthetic was achieved through meticulous production design and location scouting. The Victory community was primarily filmed in Palm Springs, California, which provided the perfect mid-century modern architecture to create the film's idealized 1950s setting. Production designer Katie Byron and costume designer Arianne Phillips worked closely to create a cohesive visual world that felt both authentic and unnervingly perfect. The vibrant color palette and precise symmetry were deliberate choices to emphasize the artificial perfection of the Victory community.

Florence Pugh's commitment to her role was evident throughout production, with the actress undergoing extensive preparation to understand her character's psychological journey. Pugh worked with a movement coach to develop Alice's physical transformation from perfect housewife to determined rebel, and her intense focus on set was noted by crew members. Despite reported tensions behind the scenes, Pugh's professional dedication never wavered, resulting in what many critics considered a career-best performance.

The film's elaborate dance sequences and complex psychological scenes required extensive rehearsal and careful choreography. The now-famous dinner party scene between Pugh and Pine was reportedly shot over multiple days to capture the precise tension and psychological warfare between their characters. Wilde's background as an actor informed her direction of these intense emotional scenes, creating space for the performers to explore their characters' complexities.
