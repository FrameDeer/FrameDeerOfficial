The Devil's Advocate (1997)

Genre: Drama, Horror, Mystery | Runtime: 144 min | Director: Taylor Hackford

Kevin Lomax (Keanu Reeves) is a brilliant young Florida lawyer who has never lost a case. His perfect record attracts the attention of John Milton (Al Pacino), the mysterious head of a powerful New York law firm. Milton offers Kevin an irresistible package: unlimited wealth, luxury, and professional prestige in Manhattan. Kevin and his wife Mary Ann (Charlize Theron) are seduced by the lavish lifestyle, but soon discover the terrifying truth about Milton's firm. As Kevin becomes increasingly entangled in defending clearly guilty clients, Mary Ann begins experiencing disturbing supernatural visions and psychological breakdowns. Kevin must ultimately confront the shocking revelation that his charismatic boss is actually Satan himself, and that he's being groomed to continue his father's work on Earth.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Taylor Hackford's supernatural legal thriller masterfully blends Faustian mythology with contemporary corporate corruption, creating a film that is both intellectually stimulating and genuinely terrifying. The Devil's Advocate succeeds as multiple genres simultaneously—a legal drama, a horror film, and a philosophical exploration of temptation and morality in the modern world.

Al Pacino delivers one of his most memorable and electrifying performances as John Milton/Satan. His portrayal is a masterclass in controlled mania, blending charm, intelligence, and malevolence in equal measure. The famous "I'm a fan of man!" monologue stands as one of cinema's great villain speeches, showcasing Pacino's ability to make evil both terrifying and seductively appealing. His chemistry with Keanu Reeves creates a compelling dynamic of mentor and protégé that drives the entire narrative.

Keanu Reeves provides the perfect moral anchor as Kevin Lomax, capturing the character's ambition and gradual moral decay with convincing nuance. His transformation from small-town lawyer to corrupted Manhattan power player feels authentic, making his ultimate moral crisis genuinely compelling. Charlize Theron, in one of her breakthrough roles, delivers a heartbreaking performance as Mary Ann, whose psychological unraveling serves as the film's emotional and horror centerpiece.

The film's production design is spectacular, with Milton's law firm serving as a perfect metaphor for modern corporate hell. The opulent, almost ecclesiastical architecture contrasts beautifully with the moral rot at the institution's core. The visual effects, particularly the surreal nightmare sequences and the final revelation of Milton's true form, remain impressive decades later.

What elevates The Devil's Advocate above typical horror-thrillers is its intelligent exploration of philosophical themes. The film raises profound questions about free will, temptation, and the nature of evil in a world where traditional morality has been replaced by corporate values. Milton's arguments about God being an "absentee landlord" and his celebration of human vanity provide genuine food for thought beyond the immediate scares.

Final verdict: A brilliantly acted, visually stunning, and intellectually provocative thriller that combines supernatural horror with sharp social commentary. Pacino's iconic performance alone makes it essential viewing, but the film's exploration of modern temptation and morality gives it lasting relevance.

The Making of The Devil's Advocate

The screenplay underwent significant development from its original concept. Based on Andrew Neiderman's novel, the script was adapted by Jonathan Lemkin and Tony Gilroy, who expanded the philosophical elements and strengthened the Faustian allegory. Director Taylor Hackford was particularly drawn to the project's exploration of temptation in contemporary corporate culture, seeing it as a modern retelling of classic morality tales.

Al Pacino's preparation for the role of John Milton was extensive. He researched various interpretations of Satan throughout literature and film, aiming to create a portrayal that was both terrifying and seductively charismatic. Pacino worked closely with Hackford to develop Milton's distinctive mannerisms and speech patterns, including the character's habit of quoting literature and philosophy. Many of Pacino's most memorable lines were reportedly improvised during filming.

The production design was crucial to establishing the film's unique atmosphere. Production designer Bruno Rubeo created Milton's law firm as a character in itself—a breathtakingly opulent space that suggests both corporate power and ecclesiastical grandeur. The firm's headquarters were filmed at the Woolworth Building in Manhattan, chosen for its Gothic architecture that perfectly complemented the story's themes.

Charlize Theron's performance required intense emotional work, particularly for Mary Ann's psychological breakdown sequences. The actress worked with a dialect coach to master a Southern accent and spent time researching the effects of isolation and psychological manipulation. Her commitment to the role's emotional demands marked a turning point in her career, demonstrating her range beyond her early modeling work.
