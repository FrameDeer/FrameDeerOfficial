Along Came Polly (2004)

Genre: Comedy, Romance | Runtime: 90 min | Director: John Hamburg

Reuben Feffer (Ben Stiller) is a straight-laced, cautious risk assessor who plays everything safe in both his career and personal life. After his new wife Lisa (Debra Messing) cheats on him with a scuba instructor during their honeymoon, Reuben's perfectly ordered world is turned upside down. He finds himself unexpectedly drawn to Polly Prince (Jennifer Aniston), a free-spirited, spontaneous childhood acquaintance who represents everything he normally avoids. As Reuben navigates the unpredictable world of salsa dancing, ferret ownership, and spicy food, he must decide whether to return to his safe but unsatisfying life or embrace the chaos and excitement that Polly brings.

FrameDeer Thoughts

John Hamburg's romantic comedy delivers exactly what you'd expect from a mid-2000s Ben Stiller vehicle: reliable laughs, cringe-worthy physical comedy, and a charming if predictable opposites-attract romance. While it may not break new ground in the genre, Along Came Polly succeeds as a perfectly serviceable comedy that plays to its stars' strengths and delivers consistent entertainment.

The film's greatest asset is the chemistry between Ben Stiller and Jennifer Aniston. Stiller excels at playing the high-strung, perpetually anxious straight man, and his portrayal of risk assessor Reuben Feffer is classic Stiller territory. Aniston brings her signature girl-next-door charm to Polly, creating a character who's free-spirited without being manic-pixie-dream-girl clichéd. Their dynamic—the ultimate control freak meets the ultimate go-with-the-flow spirit—provides the film's solid comedic foundation.

Philip Seymour Hoffman nearly steals the entire movie as Sandy Lyle, Reuben's washed-up child actor friend. Hoffman's committed performance as the inappropriate, delusionally confident former star provides the film's biggest laughs and most memorable moments. His basketball scenes and inappropriate advances toward women are comedy gold, demonstrating why Hoffman was one of the most versatile actors of his generation, even in broad comedy.

The supporting cast, including Alec Baldwin as Reuben's crude boss and Hank Azaria as the flamboyant scuba instructor, adds plenty of comedic flavor. The film follows a familiar romantic comedy structure but executes it competently, with enough genuine laughs to keep audiences entertained despite the predictable plot beats.

Final verdict: A solid, if unremarkable, romantic comedy that delivers exactly what it promises. While it won't change your life, Along Came Polly provides 90 minutes of reliable laughs and charming performances that make for perfect lightweight viewing.

The Making of Along Came Polly

Writer-director John Hamburg specifically crafted the role of Reuben Feffer for Ben Stiller, having previously worked with him on "Meet the Parents." Hamburg wanted to explore the comedy that arises when an extreme risk-averse personality is forced to confront spontaneous, unpredictable situations. The character's profession as a risk assessor was developed to logically explain his neurotic behavior while providing comedic opportunities.

Jennifer Aniston was coming fresh off her massive "Friends" success when she took the role of Polly. She brought her own experiences with spontaneous living to the character, though she admitted in interviews that she's actually more like Reuben in real life. Aniston worked with a salsa instructor for several weeks to prepare for the dance sequences, wanting to appear authentically skilled rather than relying on camera tricks.

The infamous bathroom scene where Reuben's irritable bowel syndrome is triggered by spicy food became one of the film's most memorable moments. Stiller committed fully to the physical comedy, and the scene was carefully choreographed to maximize the humor while maintaining some level of dignity. The sequence was shot over multiple days to get the timing and reactions perfect.

Philip Seymour Hoffman's character Sandy Lyle was largely improvised, with Hoffman bringing his own ideas to the washed-up child actor persona. The basketball scenes featuring Hoffman were particularly challenging to film, as Hoffman wasn't a natural athlete but wanted to perform his own basketball moves authentically. His commitment to the physical comedy, despite his lack of basketball skills, resulted in some of the film's funniest moments.
