Risky Business (1983)

Genre: Comedy, Drama, Crime | Runtime: 99 min | Director: Paul Brickman

When his parents leave for vacation, suburban Chicago teenager Joel Goodsen (Tom Cruise) decides to live it up for a week. His plans quickly spiral out of control when he damages his father's prized Porsche and needs to raise money for repairs. He meets Lana (Rebecca De Mornay), a charismatic call girl who introduces him to the world of high-end prostitution. What starts as a simple scheme to make quick money evolves into a full-scale brothel operation in his parents' house, forcing Joel to navigate dangerous clients, a suspicious pimp named Guido, and his own moral compass. The film became iconic for its exploration of teenage sexuality, materialism, and the American Dream.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Risky Business transcends its teen comedy premise to become a sharp satire of 1980s materialism and suburban values. Director Paul Brickman's debut film is far more sophisticated than its reputation suggests, using Joel's descent into prostitution as a metaphor for the compromises of capitalism and the emptiness of the American Dream.

Tom Cruise delivers a star-making performance that showcases both his charisma and dramatic range. His transformation from anxious prep school student to confident entrepreneur is completely believable, anchored by moments of genuine vulnerability. The famous underwear dance scene to Bob Seger's "Old Time Rock and Roll" became an iconic cinematic moment, but it's Cruise's ability to convey Joel's moral ambiguity that makes the performance memorable.

Rebecca De Mornay is equally compelling as Lana, bringing complexity to what could have been a stereotypical hooker-with-a-heart-of-gold role. Her character is both manipulative and vulnerable, sophisticated yet damaged. The chemistry between Cruise and De Mornay gives their relationship an authentic emotional weight that elevates the film beyond simple titillation.

The film's visual style is remarkably assured for a directorial debut, with cinematographer Reynaldo Villalobos creating a moody, atmospheric look that contrasts with the bright suburban setting. The use of Chicago's North Shore locations adds to the film's authenticity, while Tangerine Dream's electronic score perfectly captures the era's blend of anxiety and optimism.

Final verdict: A surprisingly thoughtful and stylish film that launched Tom Cruise's career while offering a smart critique of Reagan-era values. Risky Business remains relevant decades later as both an entertaining comedy and a sharp social commentary.

The Making of Risky Business

Paul Brickman's original vision for Risky Business was much darker than the final film. His script ended with Joel getting into Princeton not because of his entrepreneurial success, but despite it - a cynical conclusion about the college admissions process. The studio insisted on a more upbeat ending, leading to creative conflicts throughout production. Brickman later expressed regret about compromising his original ending.

Tom Cruise was not the first choice for Joel Goodsen. The role was offered to several established young actors including Timothy Hutton and Tom Hanks before Cruise, then known only for small roles in Endless Love and Taps, was cast. His audition impressed Brickman with its combination of all-American looks and underlying intensity. The famous underwear dance scene was largely improvised by Cruise, who wanted to show Joel's transition from inhibition to liberation.

The film's distinctive visual style was achieved through careful collaboration between Brickman and cinematographer Reynaldo Villalobos. They used available light whenever possible and favored long, fluid takes that gave scenes a naturalistic feel. The iconic train sequence, where Joel first sees Lana, was shot using actual Chicago L trains and required precise timing to capture the perfect moment of eye contact between the characters.

Tangerine Dream's electronic score was a late addition to the film. Brickman initially wanted a more traditional orchestral score but was convinced by producers to use the German electronic group. Their atmospheric music became synonymous with the film and helped establish the moody, sophisticated tone that set it apart from other teen comedies of the era.
