Mr. & Mrs. Smith (2005)

Genre: Action, Comedy, Romance | Runtime: 120 min | Director: Doug Liman

John (Brad Pitt) and Jane Smith (Angelina Jolie) appear to be a normal, upper-middle-class couple living a dull suburban life. However, each is secretly a highly skilled assassin working for competing organizations. After five or six years of marriage, the spark has faded from their relationship, but their professional lives are about to violently collide when they're both assigned to eliminate the same target. Discovering each other's true identities, they find themselves in a dangerous game of cat and mouse, trying to kill each other while rediscovering the passion that first brought them together. As their employers turn against them, they must decide whether to complete their missions or join forces against their common enemies.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Doug Liman's action-comedy delivers exactly what it promises - explosive entertainment fueled by one of the most electric on-screen pairings in modern cinema. Mr. & Mrs. Smith successfully blends marital satire with high-octane action, creating a film that's both clever and spectacular. While the plot may be straightforward, the film's real appeal lies in the undeniable chemistry between its two superstars and the creative ways it explores the metaphor of marriage as combat.

The film's greatest strength is the sizzling chemistry between Brad Pitt and Angelina Jolie, which famously translated into a real-life relationship that would dominate celebrity news for years. Their on-screen dynamic is palpable, with both actors perfectly capturing the blend of attraction and competition that defines their characters' relationship. Pitt brings his signature charm and physical comedy to the role of John, while Jolie embodies Jane with a cool, deadly elegance that makes her both intimidating and alluring. Their action sequences together are choreographed with a dance-like quality that emphasizes their connection even as they're trying to kill each other.

The action sequences are both inventive and beautifully executed, particularly the now-iconic house destruction scene where the Smiths literally tear down their suburban facade while trying to eliminate each other. Director Doug Liman brings the same kinetic energy he demonstrated in "The Bourne Identity," creating action that feels both grounded and spectacular. The film wisely balances its explosive set pieces with quieter character moments that allow the central relationship to develop beyond the surface-level premise.

The supporting cast adds considerable flavor, with Vince Vaughn providing comic relief as John's fast-talking colleague and Adam Brody making a memorable appearance as a young, overeager assassin. The production design effectively contrasts the Smiths' sterile, perfect suburban home with the chaotic, dangerous world they inhabit professionally. While the film occasionally leans into familiar action tropes, it does so with enough self-awareness and style to remain engaging throughout.

Final verdict: A thoroughly entertaining action-comedy that delivers spectacular set pieces, sharp humor, and one of cinema's most memorable pairings. While it may not break new ground in either the action or romantic comedy genres, it executes its high-concept premise with style and charisma to spare.

The Making of Mr. & Mrs. Smith

The film had a long development history dating back to the 1990s, with various directors and stars attached at different points. At one stage, the project was envisioned as a more serious thriller with Nicole Kidman and Will Smith in the lead roles. When Doug Liman came on board as director, he reimagined the concept as an action-comedy that would explore marriage through the lens of professional assassins. The script went through numerous rewrites, with Simon Kinberg making his feature film writing debut with the final version.

Brad Pitt and Angelina Jolie's casting created immediate buzz, particularly as Pitt was still married to Jennifer Aniston at the time filming began. The intense chemistry between the two stars was evident from the start, though both initially downplayed rumors of a romance. Their professional collaboration was reportedly smooth, with both actors performing many of their own stunts and working closely with the fight choreographers to create the film's distinctive action style that blended martial arts with domestic conflict.

The production faced challenges with its ambitious action sequences, particularly the climactic house destruction scene. The sequence was filmed on a soundstage with a fully constructed two-story house that could be systematically destroyed. Special effects teams used precise explosives and practical effects to create the controlled chaos of the Smiths' battle. The scene required extensive planning and multiple takes to achieve the right balance of comedy and action while ensuring the safety of the cast and crew.

Director Doug Liman brought his signature documentary-style filmmaking approach to the action sequences, using handheld cameras and natural lighting to create a sense of immediacy and realism. This contrasted effectively with the carefully composed shots of the Smiths' perfect suburban life, emphasizing the duality of their existence. The production design team created a deliberately generic, sterile home environment that would make the eventual destruction more dramatically satisfying.
