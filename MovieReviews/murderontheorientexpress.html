Murder on the Orient Express (2017)

Genre: Mystery, Crime, Drama | Runtime: 114 min | Director: Kenneth Branagh

During the lavish journey of the Orient Express through Europe, world-renowned detective Hercule Poirot finds himself embroiled in his most intriguing case yet. When a snowdrift stops the train in its tracks and a passenger is found brutally murdered, Poirot must employ his legendary "little grey cells" to solve the mystery. With all passengers confined to the train and each one a potential suspect, the detective navigates a web of lies, secrets, and hidden connections. As the investigation deepens, Poirot discovers that every passenger has a connection to a tragic kidnapping case from years past, forcing him to confront complex moral questions about justice and revenge.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Kenneth Branagh brings fresh energy to Agatha Christie's classic whodunit with this lavish and star-studded adaptation. As both director and star, Branagh delivers a Hercule Poirot that balances the character's famous fastidiousness with surprising emotional depth and physicality. His magnificent mustache alone deserves recognition for its dramatic presence throughout the film.

The ensemble cast reads like a who's who of Hollywood royalty, with each actor bringing distinct characterizations to their potentially stereotypical roles. From Michelle Pfeiffer's sultry widow to Judi Dench's imperious princess and Johnny Depp's suitably slimy gangster, the performances elevate the material beyond mere mystery tropes. The chemistry among the cast creates a palpable tension that keeps viewers guessing until the final reveal.

The production design and cinematography are nothing short of breathtaking. The recreation of the Orient Express in all its Art Deco glory, combined with stunning exterior shots of snowy landscapes, creates a visually sumptuous experience. The use of long takes and inventive camera movements, particularly during the initial investigation sequence, adds modern flair to the classic mystery format.

Where the film truly succeeds is in its handling of Christie's moral complexity. The famous resolution, which challenges traditional notions of justice, is presented with thoughtful nuance. Branagh's Poirot grapples not just with solving the crime, but with the philosophical implications of his discovery, adding layers to what could have been a straightforward detective story.

Final verdict: A handsomely mounted and thoroughly entertaining mystery that honors its source material while bringing contemporary style to the proceedings. While purists may debate certain creative choices, this adaptation successfully introduces Christie's genius to a new generation.

The Making of Murder on the Orient Express

Kenneth Branagh took a very hands-on approach to bringing Agatha Christie's most famous mystery to the big screen. He worked closely with screenwriter Michael Green to expand Poirot's backstory and add more emotional depth to the character. The opening sequence in Jerusalem, not present in the original novel, was created to establish Poirot's genius and moral compass before the main mystery begins.

The production built a full-scale replica of the Orient Express at Longcross Studios in England, allowing for elaborate camera movements and authentic period detail. The attention to historical accuracy extended to every aspect of production, from the authentic 1930s costumes to the precisely recreated dining car china. The snowy exteriors were filmed in New Zealand's Southern Alps, standing in for Yugoslavia where the novel is set.

Branagh's distinctive mustache became a character in itself, requiring extensive research and testing to achieve the perfect look. The final design was so elaborate that it required a team of three makeup artists working for hours to apply. Branagh has joked that the mustache had its own contract and required its own trailer.

The all-star cast presented scheduling challenges that required meticulous planning. Many of the big-name actors had limited availability, so their scenes had to be carefully scheduled and sometimes shot out of sequence. Despite these challenges, Branagh created a collaborative atmosphere where the ensemble could develop chemistry and play off each other's performances.
