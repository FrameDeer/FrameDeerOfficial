Sully (2016)

Genre: Drama, Biography | Runtime: 96 min | Director: Clint Eastwood

The story of Chesley "Sully" Sullenberger, who became a hero after gliding his disabled plane onto the frigid waters of the Hudson River, saving the lives of all 155 passengers and crew aboard. On January 15, 2009, Captain Sully (Tom Hanks) and First Officer Jeff Skiles (Aaron Eckhart) take off from LaGuardia Airport when their Airbus A320 strikes a flock of geese, disabling both engines. With no power and limited options, Sully makes the split-second decision to land on the Hudson River. While the world celebrates him as a hero, an investigation by the National Transportation Safety Board questions his decision, putting his career and reputation on the line.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Clint Eastwood delivers a taut, respectful tribute to real-life heroism that explores the complex aftermath of a miracle. Sully is remarkable for taking a story where everyone knows the outcome and still managing to create genuine suspense and emotional depth.

The film's narrative structure is its greatest strength, using flashbacks and nightmare sequences to explore the psychological impact of the "Miracle on the Hudson." Rather than simply depicting the heroic act, Eastwood focuses on the investigation that followed, creating a compelling drama about expertise, intuition, and the second-guessing that can haunt even the most successful outcomes.

Tom Hanks delivers a masterclass in subtle, dignified acting as Captain Sully. He captures the quiet competence and humility of the real-life pilot while also conveying the internal turmoil of a man grappling with potential career-ending consequences. His performance never veers into melodrama, instead showing the quiet strength of a professional facing unprecedented scrutiny.

Aaron Eckhart provides excellent support as First Officer Jeff Skiles, bringing warmth and camaraderie to their partnership. The chemistry between Hanks and Eckhart feels authentic, reflecting the trust and teamwork essential to aviation safety.

The actual water landing sequence is breathtakingly realistic, using a combination of practical effects and CGI to recreate the terrifying 208 seconds between bird strike and successful ditching. What makes these scenes particularly powerful is how they're intercut with the clinical simulations and hearings, highlighting the difference between theoretical scenarios and real-life human decision-making.

Final verdict: A concise, powerful drama that honors real heroes while exploring the complex nature of expertise under pressure. Eastwood's efficient direction and Hanks' nuanced performance make this a compelling examination of what happens after the miracle.

The Making of Sully

Clint Eastwood filmed Sully in an incredibly efficient 35 days, showcasing his reputation as one of Hollywood's most economical directors. The tight schedule was made possible by Eastwood's preference for minimal takes and his trust in his actors' abilities.

Tom Hanks spent considerable time with the real Captain Chesley "Sully" Sullenberger to accurately portray his mannerisms, speech patterns, and professional demeanor. Hanks even learned to fly and spent hours in flight simulators to understand the technical aspects of piloting an Airbus A320.

The water landing sequence was one of the most challenging aspects of production. The filmmakers used a combination of practical effects and CGI to recreate the dramatic Hudson River landing. They built a full-scale replica of the Airbus A320 fuselage and submerged it in a massive water tank, using hydraulic systems to simulate the plane's movement and sinking.

Eastwood insisted on using IMAX cameras for the flight sequences to enhance the immersive quality of the film. This decision paid off, as the large-format footage adds to the visceral impact of the emergency landing and rescue operations.

The real Sully Sullenberger and First Officer Jeff Skiles served as technical consultants on the film, ensuring accuracy in the cockpit procedures and emergency protocols. Their involvement helped maintain authenticity while allowing for some dramatic license in the storytelling.
