Death on the Nile (2022)

Genre: Mystery, Crime, Drama | Runtime: 127 min | Director: Kenneth Branagh

Belgian detective Hercule Poirot (Kenneth Branagh) embarks on a luxurious voyage down the Nile on the glamorous river steamer Karnak. The idyllic honeymoon of the beautiful heiress Linnet Ridgeway (Gal Gadot) and her new husband Simon Doyle (Armie Hammer) is tragically cut short when Linnet is murdered. As Poirot begins his investigation, he discovers that nearly every passenger on board had motive to kill the wealthy newlywed. With a boat full of suspects and a tightening timeline, Poirot must race against time to prevent the killer from striking again while unraveling a complex web of jealousy, revenge, and secret passions.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Kenneth Branagh's lavish adaptation of Agatha Christie's classic mystery delivers sumptuous visuals and an all-star cast in a whodunit that, while occasionally uneven, provides plenty of old-fashioned entertainment. Following his successful Murder on the Orient Express, Branagh returns as both director and star, bringing even more depth to his portrayal of the brilliant Belgian detective Hercule Poirot.

The film's greatest strength lies in its breathtaking production design and cinematography. From the opulent Art Deco interiors of the Karnak steamer to the stunning Egyptian landscapes, every frame is meticulously crafted to create a sense of 1930s glamour and exotic adventure. The costume design is particularly noteworthy, capturing the elegance of the era while reflecting each character's personality and social status.

Kenneth Branagh delivers a more nuanced and emotionally layered performance as Poirot compared to his previous outing. The film opens with a black-and-white prologue that explores Poirot's backstory and the origin of his famous mustache, adding depth to the character that goes beyond Christie's original creation. Branagh's direction maintains a steady pace, balancing character development with the intricate plot mechanics of the mystery.

The ensemble cast, while star-studded, delivers mixed results. Gal Gadot brings appropriate glamour as the ill-fated Linnet Ridgeway, while Emma Mackey stands out as the emotionally volatile Jacqueline de Bellefort. However, some performances feel underdeveloped, and the large cast occasionally struggles to establish distinct character identities amidst the crowded narrative.

Final verdict: A visually stunning and generally entertaining mystery that successfully captures the glamour and intrigue of Agatha Christie's world. While it may not reach the heights of the best Christie adaptations, it delivers a satisfying whodunit with enough twists and production value to please mystery enthusiasts.

The Making of Death on the Nile

The production faced numerous challenges including multiple release delays due to the COVID-19 pandemic and controversies surrounding cast members. Originally scheduled for December 2019 release, the film was pushed back several times before finally premiering in February 2022. The delays were compounded by the pandemic's impact on theatrical distribution and controversies involving several cast members.

Despite these challenges, the film's production design team created breathtaking sets that captured the glamour of 1930s Egypt. The Karnak steamer was built as a massive, fully functional set at Longcross Studios in England, complete with detailed Art Deco interiors and working mechanical components. For exterior shots, the production used Morocco's Ait Benhaddou to stand in for Egyptian locations, with visual effects enhancing the authenticity.

Kenneth Branagh worked closely with cinematographer Haris Zambarloukos to develop a distinctive visual style that would differentiate the film from its predecessor, Murder on the Orient Express. They employed more sweeping camera movements and grander compositions to emphasize the epic scale of the Egyptian setting. The use of 65mm film photography added to the film's lush, cinematic quality.

The costume design by Paco Delgado played a crucial role in establishing character and period authenticity. Each character's wardrobe was meticulously researched and designed to reflect their personality, social status, and emotional journey throughout the film. The attention to detail extended to accessories, jewelry, and even the specific fabrics that would have been available in the 1930s.
