1917 (2019)

Genre: War, Drama, Action | Runtime: 119 min | Director: Sam Mendes

During World War I, two young British soldiers, Schofield (George MacKay) and Blake (Dean-Charles Chapman), are given a seemingly impossible mission. In a race against time, they must cross enemy territory to deliver a message that could potentially save 1,600 of their fellow soldiers—including Blake's own brother—from a deadly German trap. Presented as a continuous "one-shot" cinematic experience, 1917 immerses viewers in the visceral reality of trench warfare and the extraordinary courage of ordinary men.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Sam Mendes' technical masterpiece redefines the war film genre through its revolutionary "one-shot" approach, creating an immersive, real-time experience that places viewers directly in the boots of two young soldiers. 1917 is not just a war movie—it's a cinematic achievement that blends technical innovation with profound emotional storytelling.

The film's single-take illusion, masterfully executed by cinematographer Roger Deakins, creates an unparalleled sense of urgency and immediacy. As we follow Schofield and Blake through trenches, across no-man's-land, and through devastated French countryside, the continuous camera work makes us feel every moment of their desperate race against time. This isn't just a stylistic choice; it's a narrative device that amplifies the emotional stakes and physical exhaustion of their mission.

George MacKay delivers a career-defining performance as Schofield, capturing the transformation from weary soldier to determined hero with remarkable subtlety and depth. His journey—from reluctant participant to driven savior—forms the emotional core of the film. Dean-Charles Chapman provides the perfect counterpoint as the optimistic Blake, whose personal stake in the mission adds layers of emotional urgency.

The supporting cast, including memorable appearances by Colin Firth, Benedict Cumberbatch, and Mark Strong, adds weight and context to the mission without distracting from the central narrative. Each cameo feels organic to the story, reminding us of the larger military structure while keeping focus on our two protagonists.

Final verdict: A groundbreaking war film that combines technical brilliance with raw human emotion. 1917 reminds us that heroism isn't about grand gestures, but about ordinary people doing extraordinary things in impossible circumstances.

The Making of 1917

The film's revolutionary "one-shot" technique was achieved through an extraordinary collaboration between director Sam Mendes and cinematographer Roger Deakins. While appearing as a single continuous take, the film was actually shot in multiple long takes ranging from 8-9 minutes each, with hidden cuts seamlessly blended through careful planning and digital effects. The longest continuous shot in the film runs for approximately 8 minutes and 30 seconds.

The production built an elaborate trench system and battlefield sets that allowed for the continuous camera movement. The sets were constructed with interconnected tunnels and passageways, enabling the camera to follow the actors through seemingly endless trenches, bunkers, and landscapes. Every element had to be meticulously planned and rehearsed for weeks before filming could begin.

Roger Deakins utilized specially modified equipment including a stabilized remote head and custom-built rigs that allowed the camera to move fluidly through impossible spaces. The camera operators practiced for months to achieve the precise movements required, often operating while running backwards or navigating complex obstacles. The technical achievement required every department—from acting to lighting to sound—to work in perfect synchronization.

The film was inspired by stories told to Sam Mendes by his grandfather, Alfred Mendes, who served as a messenger during World War I. Mendes wanted to create a film that captured the visceral, immediate experience of war rather than presenting a traditional historical epic. This personal connection to the material informed every aspect of the film's development and execution.

Fun Facts & Trivia

  • The film's trenches were built to exact WWI specifications using historical photographs and documents
  • Roger Deakins used natural light whenever possible to maintain the film's realistic aesthetic

  • Thomas Newman's score was composed to match the rhythm of the soldiers' footsteps and breathing
