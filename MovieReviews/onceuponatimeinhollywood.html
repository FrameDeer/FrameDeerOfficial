Once Upon a Time in Hollywood (2019)

Genre: Comedy, Drama | Runtime: 161 min | Director: Quentin Tarantino

A faded television actor and his stunt double strive to achieve fame and success in the film industry during the final years of Hollywood's Golden Age in 1969 Los Angeles. Rick Dalton (Leonardo DiCaprio), former star of a 1950s Western television series, struggles to come to terms with his fading career while his best friend and stunt double Cliff Booth (Brad Pitt) serves as his driver and confidant. As Rick tries to revitalize his career by playing villains in new TV shows, they navigate the changing landscape of Hollywood while living next door to rising star Sharon Tate (Margot Robbie) and Roman Polanski. The film builds toward the fateful night of August 8, 1969, when the Manson Family murders shocked the world.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Quentin Tarantino's love letter to 1969 Hollywood represents his most mature and emotionally resonant work to date. While maintaining his signature style and dialogue-driven storytelling, this film showcases a surprising tenderness and nostalgia that sets it apart from his previous films.

The film's leisurely pace and episodic structure create an immersive experience that transports viewers directly into the sun-drenched Los Angeles of 1969. Rather than driving toward a conventional plot, Tarantino focuses on character and atmosphere, allowing us to live alongside these characters during three pivotal days that would change Hollywood forever.

Leonardo DiCaprio and Brad Pitt deliver career-best performances with incredible chemistry. DiCaprio's Rick Dalton is a heartbreaking portrait of an actor confronting his own obsolescence, while Pitt's Cliff Booth embodies effortless cool and loyalty. Their friendship forms the emotional core of the film, providing both comedic moments and genuine pathos.

Margot Robbie's luminous performance as Sharon Tate serves as the film's moral and spiritual center. Her portrayal emphasizes Tate's vitality and innocence rather than focusing on her tragic fate. The scenes of her watching herself in a theater or simply enjoying life in Hollywood create some of the film's most poignant moments.

Tarantino's attention to period detail is staggering, from the meticulously recreated Hollywood Boulevard to the authentic television sets and film posters. The soundtrack perfectly captures the era, while Robert Richardson's cinematography bathes everything in golden-hour nostalgia.

Final verdict: A masterful, bittersweet fairy tale that reimagines Hollywood history with Tarantino's unique vision. Both a celebration of cinema and a poignant meditation on change, friendship, and what might have been.

The Making of Once Upon a Time in Hollywood

Quentin Tarantino's passion project was years in development, with the director describing it as his most personal film. The idea originated from his fascination with 1960s Hollywood and his desire to create an alternate history that would preserve the innocence of that era. Tarantino spent considerable time researching the period, watching old TV shows, and interviewing people who lived through that time.

The production's attention to period accuracy was extraordinary. Entire blocks of Hollywood Boulevard were transformed to their 1969 appearance, with vintage storefronts, cars, and advertisements meticulously recreated. The film used over 200 classic cars from the era, and the production team worked with historians to ensure every detail was authentic.

The casting process saw several notable changes. Initially, Tarantino envisioned the film as a starring vehicle for Brad Pitt, but the role of Rick Dalton expanded significantly when Leonardo DiCaprio expressed interest. Margot Robbie was cast as Sharon Tate after meeting with Tarantino and demonstrating her deep respect for the real-life actress.

Tarantino worked closely with the families of Sharon Tate and Jay Sebring to ensure their portrayals were respectful and accurate. The film's controversial alternate ending was carefully considered, with Tarantino wanting to create a cathartic fantasy that would allow the audience to experience a different outcome for these beloved Hollywood figures.
