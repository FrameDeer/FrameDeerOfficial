Extraction (2020)

Genre: Action, Thriller | Runtime: 117 min | Director: Sam Hargrave

Tyler Rake, a fearless black market mercenary, embarks on the most deadly extraction of his career when he's enlisted to rescue the kidnapped son of an imprisoned international crime lord. Battling overwhelming odds in one of the world's most impenetrable cities, Rake finds himself in a brutal fight for survival where the mission becomes personal. As weapons run out and allegiances shift, he must use all his skills and strength to complete the mission and make it out alive.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Sam Hargrave's directorial debut delivers some of the most visceral and technically impressive action sequences in recent memory. As a former stunt coordinator for Marvel films, Hargrave brings a fresh perspective to the action genre, prioritizing practical stunts and immersive camerawork over CGI spectacle.

The film's centerpiece is an astonishing 12-minute "one-take" action sequence that follows Tyler Rake through multiple buildings, vehicles, and combat scenarios without visible cuts. This technical marvel showcases Hargrave's background and demonstrates what happens when a stunt professional gets creative control over major action filmmaking.

Chris Hemsworth delivers a compelling performance as the tormented mercenary Tyler Rake. While the character follows familiar archetypes of the emotionally damaged action hero, Hemsworth brings genuine physicality and emotional weight to the role. His chemistry with Rudhraksh Jaiswal's Ovi provides the film's emotional core amidst the relentless violence.

The supporting cast elevates the material, with Golshifteh Farahani bringing sophistication as the mission coordinator and Randeep Hooda delivering intensity as a rival special forces soldier. The film effectively establishes Dhaka as a character itself—a chaotic, dangerous city where every corner holds potential threats.

While the plot follows conventional extraction mission tropes, the execution distinguishes Extraction from similar films. The action feels raw and consequential, with injuries having lasting impact on the characters. The cinematography by Newton Thomas Sigel creates a gritty, immersive atmosphere that complements the brutal action.

Final verdict: A masterclass in modern action filmmaking that prioritizes practical stunts and immersive sequences. While the story follows familiar beats, the technical execution and commitment to visceral action make this essential viewing for action enthusiasts.

The Making of Extraction

Sam Hargrave's transition from stunt coordinator to director marked a significant moment for action cinema. Having worked as Chris Evans' stunt double in the Marvel films and coordinated stunts for Avengers: Endgame, Hargrave brought unprecedented practical experience to the director's chair. The Russo Brothers, serving as producers, recognized his unique talent for visualizing and executing complex action sequences.

The film's legendary 12-minute "one-take" sequence required months of meticulous planning and rehearsal. While not actually a single continuous shot, the seamless editing creates the illusion of real-time action as Tyler Rake fights through buildings, escapes in cars, and navigates the chaotic streets of Dhaka. The sequence involved complex camera rigs, vehicle mounts, and precise timing from every department.

Chris Hemsworth's physical commitment to the role was extraordinary. He performed most of his own stunts, including complex fight choreography and high-risk action beats. The training regimen included extensive weapons handling, close-quarters combat, and endurance conditioning to make the character's physical prowess believable. Hemsworth's dedication to practical action aligned perfectly with Hargrave's filmmaking philosophy.

The production faced significant challenges filming across multiple countries while maintaining the gritty, authentic aesthetic. Locations in India, Thailand, and Bangladesh were used to create the fictional version of Dhaka. The international cast and crew had to navigate language barriers, cultural differences, and logistical complexities while executing some of the most technically demanding action sequences ever attempted.
