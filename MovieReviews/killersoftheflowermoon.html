Killers of the Flower Moon (2023)

Genre: Crime, Drama, History | Runtime: 206 min | Director: Martin Scorsese

Members of the Osage tribe in the United States are murdered under mysterious circumstances in the 1920s, sparking a major F.B.I. investigation involving J. Edgar Hoover. Based on David Grann's nonfiction book, the film tells the harrowing true story of the Osage Nation in Oklahoma, who became the richest people per capita in the world after oil was discovered beneath their land. This wealth attracted white interlopers who manipulated, extorted, and murdered the Osage to gain control of their money. The story follows Ernest Burkhart (Leonardo DiCaprio), a World War I veteran who falls in love with and marries Mollie Kyle (Lily Gladstone), an Osage woman, while his uncle William Hale (Robert De Niro) orchestrates a systematic campaign of murder and deception.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Martin Scorsese's epic American tragedy represents a late-career masterpiece that confronts one of the nation's most shameful historical chapters with unflinching honesty and profound moral clarity. At 80 years old, Scorsese demonstrates he remains cinema's most vital chronicler of American crime and corruption.

The film's three-and-a-half-hour runtime allows for a meticulous, novelistic approach to storytelling that immerses viewers in the Osage world while systematically exposing the machinery of greed and genocide. Scorsese and cinematographer Rodrigo Prieto create a visual language that honors Osage traditions while documenting the creeping horror of the "Reign of Terror."

Lily Gladstone delivers one of the most extraordinary performances in recent memory as Mollie Burkhart. Her quiet dignity, profound resilience, and heartbreaking vulnerability anchor the entire epic. In a film filled with masterful acting, Gladstone's work stands as a revelation that should redefine her career.

Leonardo DiCaprio and Robert De Niro bring their considerable talents to complex, morally ambiguous roles. DiCaprio's Ernest is both perpetrator and victim—a weak man caught between love and greed. De Niro's William Hale represents the banality of evil, hiding monstrous intentions behind a mask of community respectability.

What makes Killers of the Flower Moon particularly significant is its collaborative approach with the Osage Nation. Scorsese fundamentally reworked the script to center Osage perspectives, consulted extensively with tribal members, and cast Indigenous actors in authentic portrayals. This ethical filmmaking approach results in a more truthful and powerful historical reckoning.

Final verdict: A monumental achievement in American cinema that combines masterful storytelling with crucial historical reckoning. Essential viewing that will be studied and discussed for generations.

The Making of Killers of the Flower Moon

Martin Scorsese's passion project underwent significant evolution during development. Originally conceived as a FBI procedural focused on Tom White (eventually played by Jesse Plemons), Scorsese and co-writer Eric Roth completely rewrote the script after extensive consultation with the Osage Nation. They shifted the focus to the relationship between Ernest and Mollie Burkhart, centering the Osage experience rather than the law enforcement perspective.

The production's commitment to authenticity was unprecedented. Scorsese insisted on filming in the actual Osage County locations where the events occurred. The production worked closely with Osage consultants, including Principal Chief Geoffrey Standing Bear and language consultant Christopher Cote. Over 60 Osage actors were cast, and many Osage community members worked as extras and crew.

The $200 million budget from Apple TV+ represented a major investment in serious historical drama for streaming platforms. Despite the epic scale, Scorsese maintained his signature attention to period detail, from authentic 1920s costumes to meticulously recreated locations. The production built entire town sets and worked with the Osage Nation to ensure cultural accuracy in every aspect.

Leonardo DiCaprio's role changed significantly during the script revisions. Originally set to play FBI agent Tom White, he instead took on the more complex and morally ambiguous role of Ernest Burkhart. This required DiCaprio to portray a character who is simultaneously loving husband and willing participant in the conspiracy against his wife's people.
