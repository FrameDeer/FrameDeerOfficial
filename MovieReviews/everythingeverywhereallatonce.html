Everything Everywhere All at Once (2022)

Genre: Action, Adventure, Comedy, Drama, Sci-Fi | Runtime: 139 min | Directors: Daniel Kwan & Daniel Scheinert

An aging Chinese immigrant (Michelle Yeoh) is swept up in an insane adventure across the multiverse where she alone can save existence by connecting with the lives she could have led in other universes. As Evelyn Wang struggles to complete her tax audit and keep her family's laundromat business afloat, she's suddenly thrust into a cosmic conflict that spans infinite realities. Guided by versions of her husband from other dimensions, Evelyn must channel her newfound powers to fight bizarre and dangerous threats from the multiverse, including a powerful and nihilistic version of her own daughter. The film explores themes of existentialism, generational trauma, and the meaning of life through a kaleidoscopic journey that combines martial arts, family drama, and absurdist comedy in ways never before seen in cinema.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Daniel Kwan and Daniel Scheinert (The Daniels) create a cinematic masterpiece that defies categorization and redefines what's possible in filmmaking. Everything Everywhere All at Once is a breathtaking, mind-expanding tour de force that combines maximalist visual spectacle with profound emotional depth, creating an experience that is simultaneously hilarious, heartbreaking, and deeply philosophical.

The film's genius lies in its ability to balance cosmic-scale concepts with intimate human drama. While exploring infinite universes and existential threats, it remains grounded in the very real struggles of a Chinese-American family—generational gaps, cultural displacement, and the quiet desperation of unfulfilled dreams. This perfect marriage of the epic and the personal creates a film that feels both universal and deeply specific.

Michelle Yeoh delivers the performance of a lifetime as Evelyn Wang, showcasing every facet of her incredible range. She transitions seamlessly from harried laundromat owner to multiverse-hopping action hero to vulnerable mother, often within the same scene. Her ability to convey decades of regret, love, and resilience through subtle expressions makes Evelyn one of cinema's most compelling protagonists.

Ke Huy Quan's triumphant return to acting as Waymond Wang is nothing short of miraculous. He brings incredible warmth and depth to a character who could have been merely comic relief, creating a portrait of gentle strength and unwavering optimism that serves as the film's emotional anchor. Stephanie Hsu is equally brilliant as Joy/Jobu Tupaki, capturing the raw pain of generational trauma with stunning vulnerability.

The film's technical achievements are staggering—from the mind-bending visual effects that never overwhelm the story to the innovative fight choreography that incorporates everyday objects in wildly creative ways. The editing is particularly masterful, maintaining clarity while juggling multiple timelines and realities with breathtaking precision.

Final verdict: A once-in-a-generation film that reminds us why we go to the movies. Everything Everywhere All at Once is a celebration of cinema's potential to surprise, move, and transform us, proving that even in a universe of infinite possibilities, the most powerful force remains human connection.

The Making of Everything Everywhere All at Once

Everything Everywhere All at Once began as a passion project for directors Daniel Kwan and Daniel Scheinert, who spent years developing the script while working on smaller projects. The film represents the culmination of their unique creative vision, blending their signature absurdist humor with deeply personal themes about family, identity, and the meaning of existence.

The production faced numerous challenges, particularly with its ambitious scope and relatively modest budget. The Daniels and their team had to find creative solutions to realize the multiverse concept, often using practical effects and clever editing rather than expensive CGI. The film's most memorable sequences—including the raccoon universe and hot dog fingers reality—were achieved through innovative filmmaking techniques rather than massive visual effects budgets.

Michelle Yeoh's involvement was crucial to the film's success. The role of Evelyn Wang was written specifically for her, allowing the filmmakers to craft a character that would showcase her incredible range as both an action star and dramatic actress. Yeoh performed most of her own stunts, drawing on her extensive martial arts background while bringing emotional depth to every scene.

Ke Huy Quan's casting marked a triumphant return to acting after decades away from the industry. His personal journey—from child star in Indiana Jones and The Goonies to struggling actor to Oscar winner—mirrored the film's themes of rediscovery and second chances. His genuine emotion in the role added layers of authenticity to Waymond's character.

The film's editing process was particularly complex, with editors Paul Rogers working for months to balance the multiple timelines and tonal shifts. The final structure emerged through extensive testing and refinement, with the filmmakers constantly asking whether each scene served both the emotional journey and the cosmic adventure.
