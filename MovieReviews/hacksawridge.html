Hacksaw Ridge (2016)

Genre: War, Biography, Drama | Runtime: 139 min | Director: Mel Gibson

World War II American Army Medic Desmond T. Doss, who served during the Battle of Okinawa, refuses to kill people and becomes the first Conscientious Objector in American history to be awarded the Medal of Honor. Desmond Doss (Andrew Garfield) enlists in the U.S. Army to serve as a combat medic, but his deep religious convictions and personal vow prevent him from carrying a weapon or killing. Facing brutal training, court-martial threats, and constant ridicule from his fellow soldiers, Doss maintains his principles. During the horrific Battle of Okinawa at Hacksaw Ridge—a 400-foot vertical cliff—Doss single-handedly saves 75 wounded soldiers while under constant enemy fire, without ever firing a shot. His incredible courage and devotion to his beliefs make him a hero who redefines the meaning of bravery.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Mel Gibson returns to the director's chair with a powerful and visceral war drama that stands as one of the most compelling and spiritually profound films about World War II. Hacksaw Ridge is not just a war movie—it's a profound exploration of faith, conviction, and the true meaning of courage in the face of unimaginable horror. Gibson masterfully balances brutal, unflinching combat sequences with deep character development and spiritual themes.

The film's battle sequences are among the most intense and realistically portrayed in cinema history. Gibson doesn't romanticize war but instead presents its full horror with shocking clarity. The depiction of the Battle of Okinawa is relentless and chaotic, capturing the sheer terror and confusion of combat while never losing sight of the individual human stories within the larger conflict. The sound design and practical effects create an immersive experience that puts viewers directly in the heart of the battle.

Andrew Garfield delivers a career-defining performance as Desmond Doss, creating a character of immense moral strength and quiet dignity. His portrayal captures both the gentle humility and unshakable determination that defined the real Doss. Garfield perfectly embodies the paradox of a man who refuses violence yet demonstrates extraordinary courage in the most violent circumstances. His Oscar-nominated performance is both subtle and powerful, making Doss's incredible story feel authentic and deeply moving.

The supporting cast is exceptional, with Vince Vaughn delivering a surprisingly effective performance as the tough Sergeant Howell, bringing both humor and depth to his role. Sam Worthington and Luke Bracey provide strong support as fellow soldiers who initially mock Doss but come to respect his unwavering principles. Teresa Palmer brings warmth and emotional grounding as Doss's wife Dorothy, creating a touching love story that adds crucial emotional context to Doss's motivations.

The film's structure brilliantly contrasts the peaceful Virginia countryside of Doss's upbringing with the hellish landscape of Okinawa, emphasizing the transformative journey of its protagonist. Gibson's direction ensures that the spiritual themes never feel preachy but instead emerge naturally from the character's experiences and choices.

Final verdict: A masterpiece of war cinema that combines brutal realism with profound spiritual depth. Hacksaw Ridge is both a harrowing depiction of combat and an inspiring testament to the power of conviction. With outstanding performances, masterful direction, and one of the most incredible true stories ever told, this film stands as one of the greatest war movies ever made and a triumphant return for Mel Gibson as a director.

The Making of Hacksaw Ridge

Hacksaw Ridge was decades in the making, with the project going through various directors and studios before Mel Gibson came on board. The script, written by Robert Schenkkan and Andrew Knight, had been circulating Hollywood for years, with many considering Desmond Doss's story too incredible to be true. Gibson's involvement brought the necessary vision and commitment to bring this challenging true story to the screen with the appropriate scale and emotional depth.

The film's intense battle sequences were achieved through a combination of practical effects, meticulous planning, and innovative filmmaking techniques. Gibson insisted on using practical effects and real explosions whenever possible to maintain authenticity. The production built a massive recreation of Hacksaw Ridge on a farm in New South Wales, Australia, complete with trenches, bunkers, and the infamous 400-foot cliff. The attention to historical accuracy extended to the weapons, uniforms, and even the specific tactics used during the Battle of Okinawa.

Andrew Garfield's preparation for the role was extensive and transformative. He spent six months researching Desmond Doss, including meeting with Doss's family, studying his mannerisms, and reading his personal writings. Garfield also underwent intense physical training to handle the demands of the role, including military-style boot camp with the other actors. Most importantly, he worked to understand Doss's Seventh-day Adventist faith and how it informed his pacifist convictions.

The cast underwent rigorous military training led by military consultant and former U.S. Marine Captain Dale Dye. The training included weapons handling, marching drills, and living in barracks to build the camaraderie necessary for their roles as soldiers. This preparation was crucial for creating the authentic military atmosphere and relationships that ground the film's more spectacular elements.

Gibson's direction focused on creating an immersive, visceral experience that would honor both the horror of war and the spiritual journey of its protagonist. He used handheld cameras and intimate framing during battle sequences to create a sense of chaos and immediacy, while employing more classical compositions during the peaceful Virginia scenes to emphasize the contrast between Doss's upbringing and his wartime experience.
