The Gentleman (2019)

Genre: Crime, Comedy, Action | Runtime: 113 min | Director: Guy Ritchie

Mickey Pearson (Matthew McConaughey) is an American expatriate who has built a highly profitable marijuana empire in London. When word gets out that he's looking to cash out of the business, it soon triggers an array of plots and schemes from those who want to steal his domain. As various shady characters—including a ruthless tabloid editor (Eddie Marsan), a Chinese gangster (Henry Golding), and a mysterious consortium headed by Matthew Berger (Jeremy Strong)—maneuver to take over his operation, Mickey must navigate a web of treachery and violence to protect his legacy. The story unfolds through the perspective of private investigator Fletcher (Hugh Grant), who's attempting to blackmail Mickey with a salacious screenplay detailing his criminal enterprise.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Guy Ritchie's triumphant return to his British crime roots delivers everything fans have been craving since Lock, Stock and Two Smoking Barrels and Snatch. The Gentleman showcases Ritchie at the peak of his powers, blending razor-sharp dialogue, eccentric characters, and intricate plotting into a thoroughly entertaining package that feels both fresh and familiar.

The all-star ensemble cast is nothing short of magnificent. Matthew McConaughey brings quiet intensity and Southern charm to Mickey Pearson, creating a crime lord who's both sophisticated and dangerous. Charlie Hunnam shines as Raymond, Mickey's loyal right-hand man, delivering one of his most charismatic performances. But the true revelation is Hugh Grant, whose against-type turn as the slimy, scheming private investigator Fletcher steals every scene he's in. Grant clearly relishes playing against his usual romantic lead persona, creating one of the most memorable characters in recent crime cinema.

Ritchie's signature dialogue crackles with wit and style throughout the film. The verbal sparring matches are as thrilling as the physical confrontations, with each character speaking in their own distinct, colorful vernacular. The film's narrative structure—unfolding through Fletcher's increasingly elaborate screenplay pitch—allows Ritchie to play with storytelling conventions while keeping the audience deliciously off-balance.

The supporting cast elevates the material at every turn. Colin Farrell is hilarious as Coach, a martial arts instructor trying to keep his young charges out of trouble, while Michelle Dockery subverts expectations as Mickey's fiercely intelligent wife Rosalind. Henry Golding makes a compelling antagonist, and Jeremy Strong brings corporate menace to his role as a billionaire looking to buy his way into the drug trade.

Final verdict: A stylish, smart, and supremely entertaining return to form for Guy Ritchie. The Gentleman proves that when it comes to British crime capers, nobody does it better—mixing violence, humor, and sophistication in a cocktail that goes down as smooth as fine whiskey.

The Making of The Gentleman

Guy Ritchie wrote the screenplay specifically as a return to the British crime genre that made him famous. After working on big-budget studio films like the Sherlock Holmes series and Disney's live-action Aladdin, Ritchie wanted to create something more personal and closer to his original style. The script was developed with input from the cast, with many actors contributing to their characters' distinctive dialogue and mannerisms.

Casting against type was a deliberate strategy that paid off handsomely. Hugh Grant's transformation from romantic lead to sleazy private investigator was particularly noteworthy. Grant worked closely with Ritchie to develop Fletcher's distinctive speech patterns and physicality, creating a character far removed from his usual screen persona. Similarly, Michelle Dockery's casting as a tough, street-smart gangster's wife subverted expectations from her Downton Abbey role.

The film's distinctive visual style was achieved through careful location scouting and production design. Ritchie wanted to showcase both the glamorous and gritty sides of London, from Mickey's aristocratic estate to the underground fight clubs and urban landscapes. The contrast between old-money sophistication and modern criminal enterprise became a visual theme throughout the film.

The ensemble cast's chemistry was cultivated through extensive rehearsal time and improvisation sessions. Ritchie encouraged his actors to play with the dialogue and find their characters' unique rhythms. This approach resulted in the natural, overlapping conversations that have become a hallmark of Ritchie's style, with each character speaking in their own distinctive patter.
