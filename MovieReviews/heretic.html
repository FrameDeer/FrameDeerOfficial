Heretic (2024)

Genre: Horror, Thriller | Runtime: 98 min | Director: Scott Beck, Bryan Woods

Two young religious missionaries, Hugh (Hugh Grant) and Pyper (Sophie Thatcher), are sent to an isolated home in the middle of a remote swamp to confront a mysterious and dangerous heretic. What begins as a routine conversion attempt quickly descends into a terrifying fight for survival when they discover the true nature of the evil they're facing. Trapped in a labyrinthine house with a malevolent presence, they must confront their own faith and fears to escape alive.

FrameDeer Thoughts

A tense, atmospheric horror film that delivers some genuinely chilling moments despite a somewhat familiar premise. Heretic marks an interesting departure for directors Scott Beck and Bryan Woods, who previously co-wrote A Quiet Place, showcasing their ability to create suspense through environment and character dynamics.

The film's strongest element is its oppressive atmosphere. The isolated swamp setting and the labyrinthine house create a sense of claustrophobia that builds effectively throughout the runtime. The production design is particularly impressive, with the house itself becoming a characterâ€”full of hidden passages, shifting walls, and unsettling religious iconography.

Hugh Grant's against-type casting as the devout missionary Hugh is one of the film's most intriguing aspects. He brings a surprising gravitas to the role, while Sophie Thatcher provides a compelling counterpoint as the more pragmatic Pyper. Their chemistry and conflicting approaches to their mission create the film's emotional core.

Where Heretic occasionally stumbles is in its reliance on familiar horror tropes. While the execution is generally strong, some plot developments feel predictable to seasoned horror fans. The third act, while visually impressive, might divide audiences with its more explicit supernatural elements.

Final verdict: A solid, well-crafted horror entry that delivers effective scares and strong atmosphere, even if it doesn't fully break new ground in the genre.

The Making of Heretic

Directors Scott Beck and Bryan Woods conceived Heretic as a return to practical effects-driven horror after their work on larger studio projects. The film was shot on location in the swamps of Louisiana, with the production building the main house set specifically for the film to achieve the exact atmosphere they wanted.

Casting Hugh Grant against type was a deliberate choice to subvert audience expectations. Grant underwent significant preparation for the role, spending time with real religious missionaries and studying their mannerisms and speech patterns. The directors wanted his familiar charm to create an interesting contrast with the film's dark themes.

The house itself was a character in the film, designed by production designer Mara LePere-Schloop to be both beautiful and unsettling. The set featured practical moving walls, hidden passages, and shifting layouts that allowed for complex camera movements and disorienting perspectives. Much of the horror was achieved through practical effects rather than CGI.

The swamp locations presented numerous challenges for the production, including unpredictable weather and wildlife. However, the directors embraced these elements, incorporating the natural environment into the film's aesthetic to enhance the sense of isolation and primal fear.
