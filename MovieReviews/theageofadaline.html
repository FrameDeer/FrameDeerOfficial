The Age of Adaline (2015)

Genre: Romance, Drama, Fantasy | Runtime: 112 min | Director: Lee Toland Krieger

After miraculously remaining 29 years old for almost eight decades, Adaline Bowman (Blake Lively) has lived a solitary existence, never allowing herself to get close to anyone who might reveal her secret. But a chance encounter with charismatic philanthropist Ellis Jones (Michiel Huisman) threatens to dismantle the careful life she's built. Despite her reservations, Adaline finds herself falling in love, only to discover that Ellis's father (Harrison Ford) is a man from her past who once loved her decades ago. As past and present collide, Adaline must confront the life she left behind and decide whether to continue hiding in the shadows or risk everything for a chance at love and a normal life.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Lee Toland Krieger's elegant fantasy romance offers a fresh take on the immortality trope, focusing not on superheroics or epic battles, but on the quiet, heartbreaking reality of watching everyone you love grow old while you remain unchanged. The Age of Adaline succeeds as both a sweeping romance and a poignant meditation on love, loss, and what it means to truly live.

Blake Lively delivers a career-best performance as Adaline, perfectly capturing the grace and melancholy of a woman who has lived through nearly a century while remaining physically young. Her portrayal balances old-world elegance with modern sophistication, creating a character who feels authentically timeless. The subtle ways she conveys Adaline's accumulated wisdom and emotional scars elevate what could have been a simple romantic fantasy into something genuinely moving.

The film's romantic chemistry works surprisingly well on multiple levels. Michiel Huisman brings warmth and genuine charm as Ellis, making his instant connection with Adaline feel believable rather than contrived. However, the film's most powerful relationship is the unexpected reunion with Harrison Ford's William, which provides some of the most emotionally resonant moments. Ford delivers one of his most nuanced performances in years, capturing the quiet devastation of a man confronting the ghost of his first love.

The production design and costuming deserve special mention for their role in telling Adaline's story. The careful attention to period details across different decades helps visualize her journey through time, while Adaline's wardrobe—always elegant but slightly out of sync with current fashion—becomes a subtle visual representation of her eternal displacement.

Final verdict: A beautifully crafted romantic fantasy that transcends its genre conventions through strong performances and genuine emotional depth. While the scientific explanation for Adaline's condition feels unnecessary, the film's exploration of love across time makes for a compelling and surprisingly thoughtful experience.

The Making of The Age of Adaline

The screenplay by J. Mills Goodloe and Salvador Paskowitz spent years in development before finally reaching production. Originally titled "Adaline," the script went through multiple revisions to balance the romantic elements with the more profound themes of immortality and loss. The addition of a narrator helped ground the fantastical elements and provide context for Adaline's extraordinary journey through the 20th century.

Blake Lively's preparation for the role was extensive and detailed. She worked with a dialect coach to perfect the subtle changes in Adaline's speech patterns across different decades, studied historical etiquette and mannerisms, and even learned how to perform tasks in ways that would reflect someone who learned them in earlier eras. Her attention to these details helped create a character who feels authentically timeless rather than simply a modern woman in vintage clothing.

The film's visual approach was carefully designed to distinguish between different time periods while maintaining a cohesive aesthetic. Cinematographer David Lanzenberg used different color palettes and lighting techniques for flashback sequences to help audiences immediately recognize which era they were viewing. The costume design by Angus Strathie was particularly crucial, with Adaline's wardrobe serving as a visual timeline of her life while always maintaining her distinctive elegant style.

Harrison Ford's casting brought significant weight to the project. His role, while supporting, provides some of the film's most emotionally powerful moments. The scenes between Ford and Lively required delicate handling to convey decades of unresolved emotion and shared history, and both actors bring remarkable subtlety to their characters' complicated reunion.
