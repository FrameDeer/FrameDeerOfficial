Baby Driver (2017)

Genre: Action, Crime, Musical | Runtime: 113 min | Director: Edgar Wright

After being coerced into working for a crime boss, a young getaway driver finds himself taking part in a heist doomed to fail. Baby (Ansel Elgort), a music-obsessed getaway driver who suffers from tinnitus, uses personalized playlists to soundtrack his life and be the best wheelman in Atlanta. Working for mastermind Doc (Kevin Spacey), Baby must complete one last job with a crew of volatile criminals including Buddy (Jon Hamm), Darling (Eiza González), and Bats (Jamie Foxx) to pay off his debt and escape the criminal underworld. But when he falls in love with diner waitress Debora (Lily James), Baby sees a chance to drive away to a new life—if he can survive what promises to be a fatal final mission and outwit the increasingly dangerous crew he's forced to work with.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Edgar Wright delivers a cinematic tour de force that seamlessly blends high-octane action, musical rhythm, and heartfelt romance into one of the most innovative and exhilarating films of the 21st century. Baby Driver is not just a car chase movie or a musical—it's a perfectly synchronized symphony of sight and sound that represents a new frontier in cinematic storytelling. Wright's masterful direction turns what could have been a conventional heist thriller into a groundbreaking work of art where every gunshot, gear shift, and footstep moves to the beat of its protagonist's personal soundtrack.

The film's revolutionary approach to integrating music and action creates an immersive experience unlike anything else in cinema. From the opening credits sequence set to "Bellbottoms" by The Jon Spencer Blues Explosion, Wright establishes that music isn't just background noise—it's the very heartbeat of the film. Every action sequence is meticulously choreographed to specific songs, with car chases that feel like automotive ballets and shootouts that play like violent music videos. The editing by Paul Machliss and Jonathan Amos is nothing short of miraculous, creating a rhythmic flow that makes the entire film feel like one continuous, perfectly timed musical number.

Ansel Elgort delivers a star-making performance as Baby, creating a character who is both incredibly cool and deeply vulnerable. His physicality—constantly moving to the music in his earbuds—establishes Baby's unique worldview without a word of dialogue. Elgort brings a soulful quality to the role that makes Baby's desire for escape and redemption feel genuinely moving. His chemistry with Lily James is electric, creating one of the most charming and believable romances in recent action cinema. Their scenes together provide the emotional anchor that makes the high-stakes action feel personally consequential.

The supporting cast is uniformly excellent, with each criminal crew member representing a different archetype of danger. Jamie Foxx is terrifyingly unpredictable as Bats, Jon Hamm brings surprising depth to the seemingly suave Buddy, and Eiza González is magnetic as the volatile Darling. Kevin Spacey, despite the controversies surrounding him, delivers a perfectly calibrated performance as the manipulative Doc. Each character feels fully realized, adding layers of tension and complexity to what could have been simple caricatures.

The technical achievements in Baby Driver are staggering. The practical car stunts, coordinated by legendary stunt driver Darrin Prescott, are some of the most impressive ever captured on film. Unlike many modern action films that rely on CGI, Wright insisted on practical effects and in-camera tricks, giving the chase sequences a tangible, visceral quality that digital effects can't replicate. The sound design is equally innovative, with Baby's tinnitus represented through subtle audio cues that put viewers directly inside his experience.

Final verdict: A masterpiece of modern filmmaking that redefines what's possible in the action genre. Baby Driver is a perfect storm of directorial vision, technical innovation, and pure cinematic joy that deserves to be studied and celebrated for generations. It's that rare film that's both intellectually dazzling and wildly entertaining—a work of art that reminds us why we fell in love with movies in the first place. One of the most original and exhilarating films of the decade.

The Making of Baby Driver

Baby Driver began as a music video concept that Edgar Wright had been developing for over two decades before it evolved into a feature film. The director first conceived of the idea in 1994 after hearing "Bellbottoms" by The Jon Spencer Blues Explosion and imagining a car chase set to the song's frantic rhythm. Wright spent years developing the concept, creating elaborate storyboards and choreographing sequences to specific songs long before a script was even written. This unique development process—starting with the music and building the action around it—resulted in the film's revolutionary rhythmic structure.

The film's groundbreaking synchronization of action and music required meticulous planning and innovative filming techniques. Wright and his editors worked with pre-recorded playlists during the editing process, cutting scenes to match the exact beats and rhythms of each song. During filming, the cast and crew often listened to the specific tracks through earpieces to maintain the proper timing and rhythm. This approach required extensive rehearsal and precise choreography, with even the smallest gestures and movements timed to the music. The famous opening getaway sequence alone took three weeks to film and required over two dozen takes to achieve perfect synchronization.

Ansel Elgort's preparation for the role was incredibly intensive, involving months of driving training with professional stunt drivers to handle the high-speed maneuvers required for the role. Elgort learned advanced driving techniques including handbrake turns, J-turns, and precision driving at high speeds. He also worked with a dance choreographer to develop Baby's distinctive movement style, which incorporates his constant connection to music through his earbuds. Elgort's background in dance proved invaluable for maintaining the rhythmic precision required for the role.

The car stunts were achieved primarily through practical effects and in-camera techniques, with Wright insisting on minimal CGI. Stunt coordinator Darrin Prescott and his team designed elaborate chase sequences that combined precision driving with creative camera work. The production used specially modified vehicles with camera mounts that allowed for dynamic shots from within the action. The commitment to practical effects gave the chase sequences a visceral, tangible quality that digital effects often lack.

The soundtrack selection process was incredibly personal to Wright, who curated songs from his own extensive music collection. Each track was chosen not just for its musical qualities but for how it could enhance character development and narrative progression. The licensing process for the 35 songs featured in the film was complex and expensive, but Wright fought to include every track he had envisioned for specific scenes, believing that the music was as essential to the film as the script or performances.
