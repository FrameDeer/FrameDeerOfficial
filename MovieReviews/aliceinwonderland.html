Alice in Wonderland (2010)

Genre: Fantasy, Adventure, Family | Runtime: 108 min | Director: Tim Burton

19-year-old Alice Kingsleigh attends a party at a Victorian estate only to find she is about to be proposed to in front of hundreds of snooty society types. Escaping the crowd, she follows a white rabbit into a hole and returns to the magical world from her childhood, where she is reunited with her friends including the Mad Hatter, the Cheshire Cat, and others. Alice learns she is the only one who can slay the Jabberwocky, a dragon-like creature controlled by the Red Queen that terrorizes the inhabitants of Underland. In her journey to defeat the Jabberwocky and restore the White Queen to her throne, Alice must find the courage to become the champion that Underland needs.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Tim Burton brings his signature gothic whimsy to Lewis Carroll's classic tale, creating a visually stunning but narratively uneven adaptation that serves as both sequel and reimagining of the original stories. This Alice in Wonderland takes the familiar characters and settings and ages them along with its protagonist, presenting a Wonderland (here called Underland) that has grown darker and more troubled in the years since Alice's first visit. The film's greatest strength lies in its breathtaking visual design, which combines Burton's distinctive aesthetic with cutting-edge CGI to create a world that feels both fantastical and tangible.

The film's visual language is nothing short of spectacular, with Burton and his team creating a rich, textured world that blends practical sets with digital environments. From the decaying grandeur of the Red Queen's castle to the whimsical madness of the Mad Hatter's tea party, every frame is packed with visual detail and imaginative design. The character designs are particularly impressive, with the CGI creatures like the Cheshire Cat and the March Hare feeling fully integrated into the live-action world. The use of 3D technology enhances the sense of immersion, making Underland feel like a place you could step into.

Mia Wasikowska delivers a compelling performance as an older, more grounded Alice who must rediscover the wonder and courage of her childhood self. Her portrayal captures Alice's intelligence and independence while maintaining the character's essential curiosity and sense of justice. Wasikowska brings a much-needed emotional anchor to the film's more fantastical elements, making Alice's journey of self-discovery feel genuine and earned.

Johnny Depp's Mad Hatter is a fascinating creation that showcases both the actor's versatility and Burton's love for eccentric characters. Depp brings pathos and unpredictability to the role, creating a Hatter who is both entertainingly mad and tragically broken. While some critics found the performance overly mannered, it perfectly captures the character's fractured psyche and serves as the emotional heart of the film. Helena Bonham Carter is delightfully tyrannical as the Red Queen, creating a villain who is both fearsome and oddly sympathetic in her childish petulance.

Where the film struggles is in its narrative structure, which sometimes feels like a series of spectacular set pieces loosely connected by a prophecy-driven plot. The "chosen one" storyline, while serviceable, lacks the freewheeling absurdity and clever wordplay that made Carroll's original works so memorable. The film attempts to balance dark fantasy with family-friendly adventure, occasionally stumbling in its tone.

Final verdict: A visually magnificent but narratively flawed adaptation that showcases Tim Burton's incredible eye for design while struggling to capture the anarchic spirit of its source material. While it may not satisfy purists looking for a faithful Carroll adaptation, it succeeds as a spectacular fantasy adventure with memorable performances and some of the most creative visual storytelling of its decade. A feast for the eyes that occasionally forgets to nourish the heart and mind.

The Making of Alice in Wonderland

Tim Burton's Alice in Wonderland represents a unique fusion of the director's distinctive visual style with Disney's classic property. The project had been in development for years with various directors attached before Burton came on board, bringing his vision of a more mature, narrative-driven take on Carroll's world. Rather than creating a straightforward adaptation, Burton and screenwriter Linda Woolverton crafted a sequel of sorts, with a 19-year-old Alice returning to a Wonderland she barely remembers.

The film's groundbreaking visual approach combined live-action performance with extensive digital enhancement. Burton shot the entire film against green screens at Pinewood Studios, with the elaborate environments of Underland created entirely through CGI in post-production. This allowed for complete creative control over the world's design but presented significant challenges for the actors, who had to perform in largely imaginary environments. The production used a combination of performance capture, traditional animation, and live-action elements to create the film's distinctive look.

Mia Wasikowska's casting came after an extensive search for an actress who could embody both Alice's youthful innocence and her emerging strength and independence. Wasikowska, then relatively unknown, impressed Burton with her ability to convey intelligence and determination while maintaining the character's essential wonder. Her performance required extensive work with imaginary elements and complex physical choreography for the action sequences.

Johnny Depp's preparation for the Mad Hatter involved extensive research into the historical context of hatters and mercury poisoning, which informed his physical portrayal of the character's madness. Depp worked closely with costume designer Colleen Atwood to develop the Hatter's distinctive look, which incorporated elements of traditional Scottish dress and Burton's gothic sensibilities. The character's changing eye color and orange hair became signature elements of Depp's interpretation.

The film's massive commercial success was driven in part by its timing as one of the major early adopters of the 3D boom following Avatar's success. The conversion to 3D in post-production allowed Burton to enhance the film's immersive qualities, though some critics noted that the 3D effects were less integral to the experience than in films shot natively in the format.
