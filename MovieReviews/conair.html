Con Air (1997)

Genre: Action, Thriller | Runtime: 115 min | Director: Simon West

Newly paroled Army Ranger Cameron Poe (Nicolas Cage) is heading home to his wife and daughter he's never met aboard the Jailbird, a prison transport plane carrying the most dangerous criminals in the country. When the inmates, led by master criminal Cyrus "The Virus" Grissom (John Malkovich) and multiple murderer Garland Greene (Steve Buscemi), stage a violent takeover of the plane, Poe finds himself caught between his desire to get home and his duty to protect the guards and law enforcement officers on board. As the plane heads toward Las Vegas with law enforcement in pursuit, Poe must use his military training to outsmart the criminals and prevent a catastrophic disaster.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Simon West's explosive action extravaganza represents everything that made 90s action cinema gloriously over-the-top and endlessly entertaining. Con Air is a perfect storm of ridiculous premises, charismatic performances, and spectacular set pieces that knows exactly what it is and leans into it with glorious abandon. This is peak Nicolas Cage in his action hero prime, delivering one of his most memorable and quotable performances.

The film's greatest strength is its incredible ensemble cast of villains, each more colorful and entertaining than the last. John Malkovich's Cyrus "The Virus" Grissom is a masterclass in sophisticated villainy, delivering his lines with chilling precision and intellectual arrogance. Steve Buscemi's Garland Greene provides the perfect counterpoint as the quietly terrifying serial killer, creating some of the film's most unsettling and darkly comic moments. The supporting criminal cast, including Ving Rhames, Danny Trejo, and Mykelti Williamson, creates a rogue's gallery that's both threatening and darkly humorous.

Nicolas Cage delivers one of his most iconic performances as Cameron Poe, the honorable convict with a heart of gold and flowing mullet. Cage perfectly balances the character's southern charm with his lethal combat skills, creating an action hero who's both relatable and superhuman. His commitment to the role, from the Alabama accent to the emotional vulnerability, elevates what could have been a generic hero into something truly memorable.

The action sequences are spectacularly ridiculous and wonderfully executed. From the plane takeover to the Las Vegas crash landing, each set piece builds upon the last, creating a crescendo of destruction that never takes itself too seriously. The practical effects and stunt work hold up remarkably well, giving the film a tangible quality that modern CGI-heavy action films often lack.

Final verdict: A quintessential 90s action classic that delivers exactly what it promises - over-the-top entertainment, memorable one-liners, and spectacular action. Con Air understands that sometimes, you just want to watch things blow up real good, and it delivers that in spades with style and charisma to spare.

The Making of Con Air

Producer Jerry Bruckheimer's signature style is all over Con Air, from its high-concept premise to its spectacular practical effects. The film was part of Bruckheimer's incredible 90s action movie run that also included The Rock and Armageddon. Director Simon West, making his feature film debut, brought a confident visual style that balanced the film's over-the-top action with character moments that gave the ridiculous premise emotional weight.

The production utilized a real Boeing 747 for many of the exterior shots, with the plane eventually being destroyed in the film's climactic Las Vegas crash sequence. This was one of the last major films to destroy a real 747 for a crash scene, as environmental regulations and costs later made such practical effects increasingly rare. The crash sequence was filmed at the Wendover Airport in Utah, with extensive safety precautions and multiple cameras to capture the destruction from every angle.

Nicolas Cage prepared for his role by spending time with actual Army Rangers to understand their training and mindset. He also worked extensively on his southern accent and the character's physicality, including the now-iconic mullet hairstyle that became one of the film's most memorable visual elements. Cage performed many of his own stunts, bringing his characteristic physical intensity to the action sequences.

The casting of high-caliber actors like John Malkovich and Steve Buscemi as villains was a deliberate choice to elevate the material beyond typical action movie fare. Malkovich brought Shakespearean gravitas to his role as Cyrus Grissom, while Buscemi's unsettling performance as Garland Greene created one of cinema's most memorable serial killers despite limited screen time. The ensemble approach to the villain cast created a sense that every criminal on the plane was a legitimate threat.
