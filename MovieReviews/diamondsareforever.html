Diamonds Are Forever (1971)

Genre: Action, Adventure, Thriller | Runtime: 120 min | Director: Guy Hamilton

James Bond is sent to investigate a mysterious diamond smuggling operation that leads him to the reclusive billionaire Willard Whyte and eventually to his arch-nemesis Ernst Stavro Blofeld. Bond follows the diamond trail from Amsterdam to Las Vegas, where he encounters the deadly duo Mr. Wint and Mr. Kidd, and discovers Blofeld's plot to use a diamond-powered satellite laser to hold the world for ransom. Amidst the glitz and glamour of Las Vegas casinos, Bond must stop Blofeld from destroying major cities and cornering the diamond market.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Sean Connery's final official appearance as James Bond marks a significant transition in the franchise's tone and style. After the more serious and grounded "On Her Majesty's Secret Service," Diamonds Are Forever returns to the campy, gadget-heavy formula but with a distinctly American flavor that reflects its Las Vegas setting and early 1970s sensibilities.

The film represents a curious mix of classic Bond elements and emerging camp tendencies that would later define Roger Moore's era. Connery, returning after a one-film absence for a then-record $1.25 million salary, delivers a more world-weary, slightly detached performance that contrasts with his earlier energetic portrayals. His chemistry with Jill St. John's Tiffany Case provides some of the film's most entertaining moments.

The villainous duo of Mr. Wint and Mr. Kidd stands out as one of the franchise's most memorable henchman pairings. Their sophisticated, almost romantic partnership and clever murder methods add a unique flavor to the Bond villain roster. Charles Gray's portrayal of Blofeld, while controversial among fans for its campy delivery, brings a theatrical flair to the recurring antagonist.

The Las Vegas setting provides a glamorous backdrop that perfectly captures the early 1970s aesthetic. From the iconic car chase through the Vegas streets to the climactic battle on an oil rig, the film makes excellent use of its American locations. However, the plot suffers from convoluted twists and an over-reliance on spectacle over substance.

John Barry's score and Shirley Bassey's second Bond theme song maintain the franchise's musical excellence, while the production design captures the glitz and excess of Las Vegas casino culture. The film's gadgets, including Bond's moon buggy vehicle, reflect the more outlandish direction the series was taking.

Final verdict: A mixed bag that combines memorable moments with inconsistent tone. While not among the franchise's best, it remains an entertaining time capsule of 1970s Bond and an important transitional film between eras.

The Making of Diamonds Are Forever

The production of Diamonds Are Forever was marked by Sean Connery's highly publicized return to the role after George Lazenby's single appearance in "On Her Majesty's Secret Service." Connery was lured back with a then-unprecedented salary of $1.25 million plus a percentage of profits, along with financing for two films of his choice. His return was seen as crucial for restoring box office confidence after Lazenby's departure.

Director Guy Hamilton, returning after his success with Goldfinger, aimed to create a more lighthearted, American-centric Bond film that would appeal to international audiences. The choice of Las Vegas as a primary location reflected the growing American influence on the franchise and provided a glamorous backdrop that captured early 1970s excess.

The film's tone represents a significant shift from the relatively serious "On Her Majesty's Secret Service" toward the campier style that would characterize Roger Moore's era. This transition is evident in the more outlandish gadgets, including Bond's moon buggy vehicle, and the increased emphasis on humor and one-liners.

Charles Gray's casting as Blofeld created continuity issues, as he had previously appeared in "You Only Live Twice" as a different character. The production faced challenges with the Las Vegas filming, including restrictions on casino photography and the difficulty of capturing the city's unique atmosphere while maintaining the Bond franchise's sophisticated European sensibility.
