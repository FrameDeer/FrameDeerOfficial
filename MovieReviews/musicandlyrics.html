Music and Lyrics (2007)

Genre: Romance, Comedy, Music | Runtime: 104 min | Director: Marc Lawrence

Alex Fletcher (Hugh Grant) is a washed-up 80s pop star who was once part of the hit band "Pop!" Now reduced to playing nostalgia shows at theme parks and state fairs, he gets a unexpected career opportunity when reigning pop diva Cora Corman (Haley Bennett) asks him to write a new song for her. The problem: Alex is a competent musician but a terrible lyricist. Enter Sophie Fisher (Drew Barrymore), the quirky, neurotic woman who waters his plants and happens to have a natural gift for words. As they collaborate on the song "Way Back Into Love," Alex and Sophie discover creative chemistry that blossoms into romance. But Sophie's painful past and Alex's lingering insecurities threaten to derail both their budding relationship and their shot at musical redemption.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Marc Lawrence's charming romantic comedy offers a delightful behind-the-scenes look at the music industry while delivering the kind of warm, intelligent romance that feels increasingly rare in modern cinema. Music and Lyrics succeeds by combining genuine musical authenticity with the classic romantic comedy formula, creating a film that's both entertaining and surprisingly insightful about the creative process.

Hugh Grant delivers one of his most endearing performances as Alex Fletcher, perfectly capturing the mix of self-deprecating humor and lingering ambition that defines a faded pop star. His comedic timing remains impeccable, and he brings surprising depth to a character who could have been merely a caricature. Drew Barrymore matches him beat for beat as Sophie, creating a character who is both charmingly eccentric and believably vulnerable. Their chemistry feels natural and unforced, making their creative and romantic partnership completely convincing.

The film's greatest strength lies in its musical authenticity. The original songs, particularly "Pop! Goes My Heart" and "Way Back Into Love," are genuinely catchy and well-crafted, serving the story while standing on their own as enjoyable pop tunes. The attention to detail in recreating 80s music video aesthetics for the "Pop! Goes My Heart" sequence is particularly impressive, perfectly capturing the era's over-the-top production values and cheesy choreography.

Where the film occasionally stumbles is in its somewhat predictable plotting and the underdevelopment of certain supporting characters. The romantic obstacles feel somewhat manufactured, and the villainous elements are cartoonish compared to the grounded central relationship. However, these flaws are largely overcome by the strength of the lead performances and the genuine sweetness at the film's core.

Final verdict: A thoroughly enjoyable romantic comedy that offers more intelligence and musical authenticity than the genre typically provides. While it may not break new ground in terms of plot, Music and Lyrics delivers exactly what it promises: charming performances, catchy music, and a warm, satisfying romance that will leave viewers with a smile. The Making of Music and Lyrics

Director Marc Lawrence wrote the screenplay specifically with Hugh Grant in mind, having previously worked with him on "Two Weeks Notice." Lawrence was inspired by his own experiences in the music industry and wanted to create a romantic comedy that felt authentic to the songwriting process. The film's central conceptâ€”a washed-up pop star collaborating with a gifted but insecure lyricistâ€”allowed for both comedic moments and genuine emotional depth.

The musical elements were crucial to the film's authenticity. Professional songwriters Adam Schlesinger (of Fountains of Wayne) and David Abraham collaborated on the original songs, creating tracks that needed to serve multiple purposes: sound like genuine 80s hits, advance the plot, and be commercially viable. The "Pop! Goes My Heart" music video sequence required extensive research into 80s production styles, with the team studying videos from bands like Duran Duran and Wham! to capture the era's distinctive aesthetic.

Hugh Grant underwent vocal training to perform his own singing in the film, wanting to maintain authenticity in his portrayal of a former pop star. Drew Barrymore, while not required to sing, worked on developing her character's quirky mannerisms and emotional vulnerability. The chemistry between the two leads developed naturally during rehearsals and was enhanced by their shared sense of humor and professional respect.

The production filmed extensively in New York City, using locations that would enhance the romantic atmosphere while maintaining the story's musical credibility. Key scenes were shot at Carnegie Hall, Central Park, and various Manhattan apartments and recording studios. The filmmakers paid particular attention to creating authentic-looking musical environments, from the recording studio sessions to the live performance sequences.
