Inglourious Basterds (2009)

Genre: War, Drama | Runtime: 153 min | Director: Quentin Tarantino

In Nazi-occupied France during World War II, a plan to assassinate Nazi leaders by a group of Jewish U.S. soldiers coincides with a theatre owner's vengeful plans for the same. Lieutenant Aldo Raine assembles a team of Jewish soldiers to commit violent acts of retribution against the Nazis, including taking their scalps. They soon cross paths with Shosanna Dreyfus, a French-Jewish cinema owner who witnesses the execution of her family by Colonel Hans Landa. As the war rages on, both plots converge at a movie premiere where Shosanna plans to burn down the theatre with the Nazi high command inside.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Quentin Tarantino's revisionist WWII epic stands as one of his most ambitious and brilliantly executed films. Inglourious Basterds masterfully blends tense dialogue-driven scenes with explosive action, creating a cinematic experience that is both intellectually stimulating and wildly entertaining. This is Tarantino at the peak of his powers, rewriting history with style, wit, and righteous fury.

The film's structure is a masterpiece of narrative construction, weaving together multiple storylines that gradually converge toward the explosive climax. Each chapter builds tension through Tarantino's signature dialogue, creating scenes of unbearable suspense that are more thrilling than any action sequence. The opening farmhouse scene alone is a masterclass in building tension through conversation.

Christoph Waltz delivers one of cinema's most memorable villain performances as Colonel Hans Landa. His portrayal of the "Jew Hunter" is both charming and terrifying, switching effortlessly between languages and personas. Waltz commands every scene he's in, creating a villain who is as intelligent as he is menacing. His Oscar-winning performance is nothing short of perfection.

Brad Pitt brings gruff charisma to Aldo Raine, while Mélanie Laurent delivers a quietly powerful performance as Shosanna Dreyfus. The supporting cast, including Diane Kruger, Michael Fassbender, and Eli Roth, all bring their A-game to Tarantino's rich tapestry of characters. Each performance feels perfectly calibrated to the film's unique tone.

The film's production design and cinematography create a lush, cinematic world that feels both authentic and heightened. Ennio Morricone's score, combined with Tarantino's eclectic soundtrack choices, elevates every scene. The final act set in the cinema is pure cinematic magic, blending film history with explosive revenge fantasy.

Final verdict: A bold, brilliant, and endlessly rewatchable masterpiece that showcases Tarantino's unique vision at its most potent. Essential viewing for anyone who loves cinema.

The Making of Inglourious Basterds

Quentin Tarantino spent nearly a decade developing Inglourious Basterds, originally conceiving it as a more straightforward war film inspired by spaghetti westerns and French cinema. The script evolved significantly over time, with Tarantino eventually deciding to embrace his revisionist history approach, creating what he called a "spaghetti western but with World War II iconography."

The casting of Christoph Waltz was crucial to the film's success. Tarantino had written the role of Hans Landa specifically requiring an actor who could speak German, French, English, and Italian fluently. After seeing numerous actors struggle with the role, he found Waltz, who not only mastered the languages but brought the character to life in ways Tarantino hadn't imagined. The director later stated that without Waltz, he might have abandoned the project.

The film's multilingual approach was groundbreaking for a major Hollywood production. About 70% of the dialogue is in languages other than English, with Tarantino insisting on authenticity rather than having everyone speak accented English. This decision created unprecedented challenges but ultimately paid off, making the film feel more authentic and immersive.

The production design meticulously recreated 1940s France and Germany, with particular attention paid to the cinema sequences. Tarantino studied actual Nazi propaganda films to create the fictional "Nation's Pride" film within the movie. The final theatre explosion was achieved through a combination of practical effects and CGI, creating one of cinema's most cathartic climaxes.

Fun Facts & Trivia

  • Brad Pitt's character is named after Aldo Ray, a 1950s Hollywood actor
  • The film's title is intentionally misspelled, inspired by a 1978 Italian film
  • Quentin Tarantino plays the scalped Nazi in the opening scene
  • Mike Myers was cast specifically because Tarantino wanted "a British actor playing a British actor playing a British general"
  • The film features over 30 minutes of footage in French and German
