Cars 3 (2017)

Genre: Animation, Adventure, Comedy | Runtime: 102 min | Director: Brian Fee

Blindsided by a new generation of blazing-fast rookie cars, the legendary Lightning McQueen (Owen Wilson) finds himself pushed out of the sport he loves. To get back in the game, he needs the help of an eager young race technician, Cruz Ramirez (Cristela Alonzo), who has her own plans for winning. With inspiration from the late Fabulous Hudson Hornet and a few unexpected turns, No. 95 prepares to compete on Piston Cup Racing's biggest stage. McQueen must prove to a new generation of racers, including high-tech rookie Jackson Storm (Armie Hammer), that he's still the best race car in the world. This emotional journey takes McQueen back to his roots, where he discovers that while he may not be the car he once was, he can still be the champion he was born to be by helping others achieve their dreams.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Brian Fee's heartfelt trilogy conclusion represents a triumphant return to form for the Cars franchise, delivering the emotional depth and character-driven storytelling that made the original so beloved while addressing mature themes of aging, legacy, and passing the torch. Cars 3 successfully course-corrects from the spy adventure excesses of its predecessor, returning the focus to Lightning McQueen's personal journey and the soul of racing that defined the series' beginning.

The film's greatest strength lies in its poignant exploration of aging and relevance in a rapidly changing world. Lightning McQueen's struggle to compete against a new generation of high-tech racers led by the formidable Jackson Storm creates a powerful metaphor for dealing with obsolescence and finding new purpose. Owen Wilson delivers his most nuanced vocal performance as McQueen, capturing the character's vulnerability, frustration, and eventual wisdom with genuine emotional depth.

Cristela Alonzo's Cruz Ramirez emerges as the film's emotional heart and one of Pixar's most compelling new characters in years. Her journey from enthusiastic but inexperienced trainer to confident racer provides a beautiful parallel to McQueen's own arc. The mentorship dynamic that develops between them evolves naturally into a partnership of equals, creating one of the franchise's most meaningful relationships.

The film's visual style represents Pixar's most sophisticated automotive animation to date. The racing sequences are breathtaking in their intensity and realism, with particular attention paid to the physics of dirt track racing and the visceral impact of high-speed crashes. The dramatic color palette and lighting choices enhance the film's emotional tone, from the nostalgic golden hues of flashback sequences to the stark, clinical environment of the modern racing facility.

Where Cars 3 truly excels is in its thoughtful handling of legacy and mentorship. The ghost of Doc Hudson looms large over the narrative, with McQueen's journey mirroring his mentor's own experiences with aging out of the sport he loved. The film beautifully explores how true greatness isn't just about personal achievement but about inspiring the next generation—a theme that gives the trilogy a satisfying and emotionally resonant conclusion.

Final verdict: A mature, emotionally rich conclusion to the Cars trilogy that successfully blends exciting racing action with thoughtful themes about legacy, mentorship, and finding new purpose. Cars 3 proves that even franchises can grow up gracefully, delivering Pixar's signature emotional storytelling while honoring what made the original so special.

The Making of Cars 3

The creative approach for Cars 3 was consciously designed as a course correction following the mixed reception of Cars 2. First-time director Brian Fee, who had worked as a storyboard artist on the first two films, wanted to return to the emotional core of the original while exploring more mature themes that would resonate with audiences who had grown up with the franchise. The concept of McQueen facing his own mortality in the sport he loved emerged as the central theme.

Extensive research went into capturing the authenticity of different racing environments. The animation team studied demolition derbies, dirt track racing, and modern racing technology to create distinct visual styles for each racing discipline featured in the film. Particular attention was paid to the physics of dirt track racing, with animators creating new simulation techniques to realistically depict how cars interact with dirt surfaces at high speeds.

The development of Cruz Ramirez represented a significant step forward for female representation in the Cars universe. Cristela Alonzo worked closely with the writers to ensure Cruz felt authentic and multidimensional, bringing her own experiences to the character's journey from self-doubt to confidence. The decision to make Cruz Latina was intentional, with the creative team wanting to reflect the diversity of real-world racing fans and participants.

Visual storytelling played a crucial role in conveying McQueen's emotional journey. The film uses color and lighting to distinguish between different eras and emotional states—the warm, golden tones of flashback sequences contrast with the cold, sterile environment of the modern racing facility. The dramatic crash sequence that opens the film was carefully choreographed to establish the stakes and McQueen's vulnerability without being too intense for younger viewers.
