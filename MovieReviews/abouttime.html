About Time (2013)

Genre: Romance, Comedy, Fantasy | Runtime: 123 min | Director: Richard Curtis

At the age of 21, Tim Lake (Domhnall Gleeson) discovers he can travel in time. His father (Bill Nighy) reveals that the men in their family have always had this ability to revisit any moment in their own past. Tim decides to use his gift to improve his life by winning the heart of the beautiful American Mary (Rachel McAdams). However, as his journey progresses, Tim learns that even time travel has its limitations and consequences. The film follows Tim through the years as he uses his extraordinary ability to navigate love, family, career, and the bittersweet moments that make life worth living, ultimately learning that the secret to happiness isn't about changing the past, but about appreciating the present.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Richard Curtis's most personal and profound film transcends the romantic comedy genre to become a moving meditation on life, love, and the preciousness of ordinary moments. While marketed as a time-travel romance, About Time reveals itself to be a deeply philosophical exploration of how we choose to live our lives and appreciate the people we love.

The film's genius lies in its subversion of time-travel tropes. Rather than using his ability for grand historical changes or personal gain, Tim employs time travel for the most human of purposes: to correct social awkwardness, to perfect romantic moments, and to spend more time with loved ones. This grounded approach makes the fantastical premise feel remarkably relatable and emotionally resonant.

Domhnall Gleeson delivers a career-defining performance as Tim, bringing an endearing awkwardness and genuine warmth to the role. His transformation from an insecure young man to a confident husband and father feels authentic and earned. Rachel McAdams brings her signature charm to Mary, creating a love interest who is both idealized and realistically flawed.

Bill Nighy's performance as Tim's father provides the film's emotional anchor. Their relationship evolves beautifully throughout the story, culminating in one of the most poignant father-son scenes in modern cinema. The film's ultimate message about savoring everyday life emerges naturally from their interactions, making the philosophical themes feel earned rather than preachy.

The supporting cast, including Tom Hollander as the cynical playwright Harry and Lydia Wilson as Tim's troubled sister Kit Kat, adds depth and humor to the narrative. Each character serves to explore different aspects of Tim's journey and the film's central themes about choice, consequence, and contentment.

Final verdict: A deceptively simple film that grows richer with each viewing. About Time starts as a charming romance but deepens into a profound reflection on life's fleeting beauty, making it Richard Curtis's most mature and emotionally satisfying work.

The Making of About Time

Richard Curtis conceived About Time as what he announced would be his final film as director, wanting to create a more personal story that reflected his own experiences with family and fatherhood. The time-travel element emerged as a metaphor for the universal desire to relive precious moments and correct mistakes, while the father-son relationship became the emotional core of the story.

Casting Domhnall Gleeson as Tim was crucial to the film's success. Curtis wanted an actor who could convey both intellectual curiosity and emotional vulnerability. Gleeson's natural charm and everyman quality made the extraordinary premise feel grounded and relatable. Rachel McAdams brought her signature warmth to Mary, creating chemistry with Gleeson that felt authentic and developed naturally throughout their characters' relationship.

The time-travel mechanics were deliberately kept simple and rule-based to serve the story rather than dominate it. Curtis established clear limitations: Tim could only travel within his own lifetime, couldn't change events before his children were conceived, and needed a dark, quiet space to time travel. These rules created stakes and prevented the time travel from becoming an easy solution to every problem.

The film's visual style emphasized the beauty of ordinary moments. Director of photography John Guleserian used natural lighting and intimate framing to create a warm, nostalgic atmosphere. The Cornwall coastline provided a stunning backdrop for the family home, representing both timeless beauty and the preciousness of family traditions.
