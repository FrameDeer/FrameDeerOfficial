The Menu (2022)

Genre: Horror, Comedy, Thriller | Runtime: 107 min | Director: Mark Mylod

A young couple travels to a remote island to eat at an exclusive restaurant where the acclaimed chef has prepared a lavish menu with some shocking surprises. Margot, a last-minute date for wealthy foodie Tyler, joins an elite group of diners including food critics, wealthy regulars, and celebrity chefs at Hawthorne, a temple of molecular gastronomy run by the mysterious Chef Slowik. As each meticulously crafted course is served, the evening takes increasingly dark and violent turns, revealing that the wealthy patrons are about to get more than they bargained for in this satirical thriller that skewers foodie culture, class inequality, and the pretensions of high-end dining.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Mark Mylod's deliciously dark satire serves up a perfect blend of horror, comedy, and social commentary that skewers foodie culture with surgical precision. The Menu represents that rare film that understands its targets so completely it can dismantle them with both savage wit and genuine insight, creating an experience that's simultaneously horrifying, hilarious, and thought-provoking.

The film's brilliance lies in its meticulous structure, mirroring the multi-course tasting menu it depicts. Each course serves as both a plot development and a thematic statement, with Chef Slowik's culinary creations becoming increasingly elaborate metaphors for the sins of his guests. From the "breadless bread plate" that comments on empty extravagance to the devastating "mess" course that reveals hidden truths, every dish advances both narrative and character while delivering biting social commentary.

Ralph Fiennes delivers a masterful performance as Chef Slowik, creating a character who is simultaneously terrifying, pathetic, and strangely sympathetic. His quiet intensity and culinary fundamentalism make him one of the most memorable antagonists in recent cinema. The way he shifts between charming host, artistic visionary, and cold-blooded cult leader is mesmerizing to watch, particularly in his interactions with Anya Taylor-Joy's Margot.

Anya Taylor-Joy continues to demonstrate her remarkable range as Margot, the unexpected wild card who sees through the pretension and recognizes the danger before anyone else. Her chemistry with Nicholas Hoult's perfectly obnoxious Tyler creates both comedic tension and genuine suspense, while the supporting cast—including Judith Light, John Leguizamo, and Hong Chau as the terrifying maître d' Elsa—adds layers of satire and menace to the increasingly dire situation.

Final verdict: A brilliantly crafted satire that serves as both an entertaining thriller and a sharp critique of culinary elitism and class inequality. The Menu satisfies on every level, delivering laughs, shocks, and food for thought in equal measure.

The Making of The Menu

The film's development began with screenwriters Seth Reiss and Will Tracy drawing from their experiences writing for The Onion and their fascination with high-end culinary culture. They conceived the story as a dark satire that would expose the absurdities of foodie pretension while delivering genuine thrills. Director Mark Mylod, known for his work on Succession, was attracted to the material's sharp social commentary and its unique blend of horror and comedy.

Ralph Fiennes underwent extensive culinary training to prepare for his role as Chef Slowik, working with professional chefs to learn proper knife skills and kitchen etiquette. His dedication to authenticity extended to actually preparing some of the dishes seen in the film. The culinary team, led by food stylist and chef Dominique Crenn, created visually stunning dishes that were both beautiful to look at and thematically relevant to the story.

The production design was crucial in creating the specific atmosphere of Hawthorne restaurant. The team designed a minimalist, almost monastic space that reflected Chef Slowik's artistic severity and cult-like control over his domain. The open kitchen layout allowed for dramatic staging of the culinary performances, while the remote island setting enhanced the sense of isolation and inescapability.

Filming during COVID-19 restrictions presented unique challenges, particularly for the dinner party scenes that required the entire cast to be in close proximity. The production implemented strict safety protocols while maintaining the intimate, claustrophobic atmosphere essential to the story. The result was a tightly controlled set that ironically mirrored the controlled environment of Hawthorne itself.
