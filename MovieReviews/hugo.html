Hugo (2011)

Genre: Adventure, Drama, Family | Runtime: 126 min | Director: Martin Scorsese

Set in 1930s Paris, Hugo (Asa Butterfield) is an orphan living in the walls of a train station, where he maintains the station's clocks while avoiding the station inspector (Sacha Baron Cohen). Hugo's only connection to his late father (Jude Law) is an automaton that doesn't work without a special key. When Hugo crosses paths with a toy shop owner (Ben Kingsley) and his goddaughter Isabelle (Chloë Grace Moretz), he discovers they hold the key to not only fixing the automaton but also to unlocking a secret that connects to the early days of cinema. The mystery leads them to rediscover the forgotten work of pioneering filmmaker Georges Méliès, celebrating the magic of cinema and the importance of preserving film history.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Martin Scorsese's love letter to cinema is a breathtaking achievement that combines cutting-edge 3D technology with a deeply heartfelt story about the magic of movies. Hugo represents Scorsese's first foray into family-friendly filmmaking, but it retains all the visual sophistication and emotional depth of his more adult-oriented works. The film is both a thrilling adventure and a moving tribute to the pioneers who created the art form we know today.

The film's greatest triumph is its revolutionary use of 3D technology. Unlike many films that use 3D as a gimmick, Scorsese employs it as an artistic tool to create depth, intimacy, and wonder. From the intricate clockwork mechanisms to the sweeping shots of 1930s Paris, every frame is meticulously crafted to immerse viewers in Hugo's world. The 3D enhances the storytelling rather than distracting from it, creating one of the few genuinely essential 3D cinematic experiences.

Asa Butterfield delivers a remarkable performance as Hugo, capturing both the character's technical genius and his emotional vulnerability. His large, expressive eyes convey volumes of emotion, making Hugo's journey from lonely orphan to hopeful adventurer both believable and deeply moving. Chloë Grace Moretz provides the perfect counterpart as the bookish, adventurous Isabelle, and their chemistry creates a charming central relationship that drives the narrative forward.

Ben Kingsley gives one of his most nuanced performances as Georges Méliès, the real-life pioneering filmmaker whose story forms the emotional core of the film's second half. Kingsley masterfully portrays Méliès' transformation from bitter, forgotten artist to rediscovered visionary, creating moments of genuine poignancy that celebrate the importance of artistic preservation. The supporting cast, including Sacha Baron Cohen's surprisingly touching station inspector and Jude Law's brief but memorable appearance as Hugo's father, adds richness and texture to the world.

Final verdict: A cinematic masterpiece that works on multiple levels - as a thrilling adventure for children, a moving drama for adults, and an essential history lesson for film lovers. Hugo reminds us why we fell in love with movies in the first place and why preserving their history matters.

The Making of Hugo

Martin Scorsese's passion project was adapted from Brian Selznick's acclaimed 2007 novel "The Invention of Hugo Cabret," which combined elements of traditional novels and picture books. Scorsese was drawn to the story because of its celebration of early cinema and its themes of preservation and restoration, which mirrored his own work with The Film Foundation. The director saw the film as an opportunity to introduce younger audiences to the magic of early filmmakers like Georges Méliès.

The production utilized groundbreaking 3D technology in ways that had never been attempted before. Scorsese worked closely with cinematographer Robert Richardson to develop a 3D aesthetic that served the story rather than simply providing spectacle. They used the technology to create intimacy in close-ups, depth in the intricate clockwork sequences, and wonder in the recreations of Méliès' films. The train station set was built on soundstages at London's Shepperton Studios, with meticulous attention to period detail and practical effects.

The recreation of Georges Méliès' studio and films was a particular labor of love for Scorsese. The production team studied surviving photographs and documents to accurately rebuild Méliès' glass studio in Montreuil, France. They also recreated several of Méliès' most famous films, including "A Trip to the Moon," using the same techniques and camera angles as the original filmmaker. These sequences required extensive research and painstaking attention to detail to capture the authentic look and feel of early 20th-century filmmaking.

Asa Butterfield was chosen from thousands of young actors who auditioned for the role of Hugo. Scorsese was impressed by Butterfield's ability to convey complex emotions with minimal dialogue, particularly through his expressive eyes. The young actor underwent extensive training to learn how to operate the complex clock mechanisms shown in the film, adding authenticity to his performance as the station's secret timekeeper.
