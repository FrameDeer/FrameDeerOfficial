Hereditary (2018)

Genre: Horror, Drama | Runtime: 127 min | Director: Ari Aster

After the family matriarch passes away, a grieving family is haunted by tragic and disturbing occurrences, and begin to unravel dark secrets. The Graham family is still dealing with the recent death of Ellen, the secretive grandmother. Annie, a miniature artist, tries to cope with her mother's complex legacy while her husband Steve and their two children, Peter and Charlie, each deal with their grief in different ways. When another tragedy strikes the family, Annie's search for answers leads her to discover a sinister fate that seems to be inherited from her mother's side.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Ari Aster's feature debut is a masterclass in atmospheric horror that redefines what the genre can achieve. Hereditary transcends traditional horror tropes to deliver a devastating family drama wrapped in supernatural terror, creating one of the most unsettling and emotionally brutal horror films ever made.

The film's power lies in its meticulous pacing and relentless building of dread. Aster masterfully controls every frame, using composition, sound design, and subtle visual cues to create an atmosphere of overwhelming anxiety. Unlike many modern horror films that rely on jump scares, Hereditary earns its terror through psychological tension and emotional devastation.

Toni Collette delivers what may be the greatest performance in horror film history as Annie Graham. Her portrayal of a mother unraveling under the weight of grief, guilt, and supernatural forces is nothing short of breathtaking. The raw emotional intensity she brings to scenes like the dinner table confrontation and support group monologue elevates the film to Shakespearean tragedy.

Alex Wolff's transformation as Peter is equally remarkable, capturing the profound trauma and dissociation of a teenager grappling with unimaginable guilt. Milly Shapiro creates one of cinema's most unsettling child characters as Charlie, whose peculiar mannerisms and artistic creations hint at the family's dark inheritance.

The film's production design is extraordinary, with Annie's miniature artworks serving as a brilliant metaphor for the family's lack of control over their own lives. The sound design creates a constant sense of unease, from the subtle clicks of Charlie's tongue to the overwhelming silence that follows each tragedy.

Final verdict: A monumental achievement in horror cinema that will haunt you long after the credits roll. Essential viewing for those who appreciate horror as art, though not for the faint of heart.

The Making of Hereditary

Ari Aster developed Hereditary over several years, originally conceiving it as a family tragedy that gradually reveals itself as a supernatural horror film. The script went through numerous revisions, with Aster carefully balancing the family drama elements with the escalating horror to create his desired effect of "a tragedy that curdles into a nightmare."

The film's distinctive visual style was heavily influenced by Aster's background in short films and his love of classic horror cinema. He worked closely with cinematographer Pawel Pogorzelski to create a look that felt both naturalistic and deeply unsettling. The camera often remains static or moves slowly, allowing scenes to play out in long takes that maximize emotional impact.

Toni Collette's casting was essential to the film's success. Aster wrote the role with her in mind, knowing he needed an actress capable of navigating the character's extreme emotional range. Collette immersed herself in the role, spending time with real miniature artists and undergoing intense emotional preparation for the film's most demanding scenes.

The miniature houses and artworks were created by a team of artists working for months before filming began. These miniatures served both as practical sets for certain shots and as powerful visual metaphors for the family's lack of agency. The sound design was equally meticulous, with every click, breath, and whisper carefully crafted to create unease.
