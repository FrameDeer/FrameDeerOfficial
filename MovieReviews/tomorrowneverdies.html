Tomorrow Never Dies (1997)

Genre: Action, Adventure, Thriller | Runtime: 119 min | Director: Roger Spottiswoode

James Bond returns to prevent World War III in a plot orchestrated by media mogul Elliot Carver, who seeks to manipulate global events for ratings and political power. When a British warship disappears in Chinese waters, Bond investigates Carver's media empire and discovers his plan to provoke war between China and the UK for exclusive broadcasting rights. Teaming up with Chinese agent Wai Lin, Bond races from Germany to Vietnam to stop Carver from triggering a global conflict, using everything from remote-controlled cars to high-tech stealth boats in his most technologically advanced mission yet.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Tomorrow Never Dies represents Pierce Brosnan's second outing as James Bond, refining the modern take on the character while delivering spectacular action and a surprisingly prescient villain. Released during the early internet age, the film's themes of media manipulation and information warfare feel remarkably relevant decades later.

The film's greatest strength lies in its forward-thinking premise. Elliot Carver as a media mogul seeking to create news rather than report it was ahead of its time, anticipating modern concerns about fake news and media manipulation. Jonathan Pryce's performance strikes the perfect balance between corporate sophistication and megalomaniacal madness, creating one of the franchise's most memorable modern villains.

Michelle Yeoh's Wai Lin stands as one of the most capable and independent Bond women in the series' history. As a highly trained Chinese agent, she's Bond's equal in combat and espionage, representing a significant evolution from earlier female characters. Their partnership feels genuinely collaborative, with Yeoh bringing impressive martial arts skills and strong screen presence that elevates every scene she's in.

Pierce Brosnan settles comfortably into the Bond role, blending the character's traditional sophistication with modern physicality. His chemistry with both Yeoh and Teri Hatcher creates compelling dynamics, though the latter's role feels somewhat underdeveloped. Brosnan handles the film's extensive action sequences with convincing physicality while maintaining the character's signature wit and charm.

The action sequences are consistently impressive, particularly the spectacular pre-title sequence and the motorcycle chase through Saigon. The film embraces 90s technology with inventive gadgets while maintaining the classic Bond formula. David Arnold's score successfully bridges classic Bond themes with contemporary action scoring, paying homage to John Barry while establishing his own voice.

Final verdict: A solid, entertaining Bond adventure that successfully modernizes the franchise while delivering spectacular action and a prescient villain. Brosnan's confident performance and Yeoh's standout role make this a memorable entry.

The Making of Tomorrow Never Dies

Tomorrow Never Dies faced a rushed production schedule following the massive success of GoldenEye. With only two years between films instead of the usual three, the production team worked under significant pressure to deliver another hit. The original title, "Tomorrow Never Lies," was accidentally changed during a phone conversation and the new title stuck.

The film's most technically ambitious sequence, the remote-controlled car chase, required innovative special effects and practical engineering. BMW provided several 750iL sedans modified for remote operation, with the filmmakers developing new techniques for high-speed precision driving. The scene where Bond controls his car from the back seat using a mobile phone showcased cutting-edge 90s technology while maintaining the franchise's gadget tradition.

Michelle Yeoh's casting as Wai Lin represented a significant step forward for representation in Bond films. Already an established action star in Hong Kong cinema, Yeoh performed most of her own stunts despite the risks. Her martial arts background brought authentic physicality to the role, and her chemistry with Brosnan created one of the franchise's most equal partnerships. The motorcycle chase through Saigon, with both actors handcuffed together, required extensive coordination and trust between the performers.

David Arnold's score marked the beginning of a new era for Bond music. As a lifelong Bond fan, Arnold successfully blended John Barry's classic themes with contemporary electronic elements, creating a sound that honored tradition while feeling modern. His work would define the musical direction of the franchise for the next decade.

The film's prescient themes about media manipulation were developed during early internet expansion. Screenwriter Bruce Feirstein drew inspiration from real media moguls and emerging concerns about information warfare. While some elements felt exaggerated in 1997, many aspects have become increasingly relevant in the age of social media and digital misinformation.
