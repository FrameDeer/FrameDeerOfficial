The Departed (2006)

Genre: Crime, Drama, Thriller | Runtime: 151 min | Director: Martin Scorsese

An undercover cop and a mole in the police attempt to identify each other while infiltrating an Irish gang in South Boston. In this intense cat-and-mouse game, young cop Billy Costigan (Leonardo DiCaprio) goes undercover to infiltrate the organization of gangland chief Frank Costello (Jack Nicholson). Simultaneously, Costello's protégé Colin Sullivan (Matt Damon) has infiltrated the Massachusetts State Police as an informer for the same syndicate. When both sides realize they have a mole in their midst, Sullivan and Costigan must race to uncover each other's identity before being exposed. The tension escalates as each man becomes deeply consumed by their double life, blurring the lines between their real identities and their undercover personas.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Martin Scorsese's masterful crime thriller represents a triumphant return to form and the film that finally earned him his long-overdue Academy Award for Best Director. The Departed combines Scorsese's signature gritty realism with razor-sharp storytelling and one of the most impressive ensemble casts ever assembled.

The film's intricate dual narrative structure creates unbearable tension as both moles navigate their increasingly dangerous double lives. William Monahan's brilliant screenplay adapts the Hong Kong thriller "Infernal Affairs" while thoroughly Americanizing it, transforming the story into a distinctly Bostonian tale of corruption, loyalty, and identity.

Leonardo DiCaprio delivers one of his most raw and emotionally charged performances as Billy Costigan, a man torn between his duty and his crumbling sanity. His portrayal of a undercover cop losing his grip on reality is both heartbreaking and terrifying. Meanwhile, Matt Damon perfectly captures the smooth, calculating nature of a man who has completely embraced his deception.

Jack Nicholson's Frank Costello stands as one of cinema's most memorable gangsters—charismatic, unpredictable, and utterly terrifying. The supporting cast is equally phenomenal, with Mark Wahlberg, Martin Sheen, and Alec Baldwin delivering career-best work. Vera Farmiga provides the moral and emotional center as the woman caught between both men.

Scorsese's direction is at its most confident, balancing explosive violence with intimate character moments. The film's Boston setting becomes a character in itself, with Scorsese capturing the city's unique atmosphere and tribal loyalties. The editing creates relentless momentum, while the soundtrack perfectly complements the narrative's escalating tension.

Final verdict: A flawless crime thriller that represents Scorsese at the peak of his powers. Essential viewing that combines masterful storytelling with unforgettable performances.

The Making of The Departed

Martin Scorsese's adaptation of the Hong Kong thriller "Infernal Affairs" underwent significant development to transform it into a distinctly American story. Screenwriter William Monahan relocated the action to Boston, drawing on the city's rich history of Irish-American organized crime and political corruption. The Boston setting became integral to the film's identity, with local dialects, neighborhoods, and cultural references adding authentic texture.

The casting process brought together an extraordinary ensemble. Leonardo DiCaprio and Matt Damon were cast as the dual protagonists, marking DiCaprio's fourth collaboration with Scorsese. Jack Nicholson's involvement as Frank Costello required significant script changes to accommodate his iconic persona. Nicholson famously improvised several of his most memorable lines, including the "microprocessors" monologue.

The production faced challenges with Boston's unpredictable weather and the logistical complexity of shooting in authentic locations. Scorsese insisted on filming in real Boston neighborhoods rather than soundstages, capturing the city's unique atmosphere. The cast underwent extensive training with Boston police and FBI consultants to ensure authenticity in their portrayals.

Scorsese's direction emphasized the psychological toll of undercover work, with particular focus on DiCaprio's character's deteriorating mental state. The famous "rat" symbolism was Scorsese's addition, appearing throughout the film to underscore themes of betrayal and identity. The director's meticulous attention to detail extended to every aspect of production, from costume design to the carefully curated soundtrack.
