Goodfellas (1990)

Genre: Crime, Drama, Biography | Runtime: 146 min | Director: Martin Scorsese

The story of Henry Hill (Ray Liotta), a half-Irish, half-Sicilian Brooklyn kid who grows up idolizing the mobsters in his neighborhood and becomes one of them. As he climbs the ranks of organized crime alongside his ruthless partners Jimmy Conway (Robert De Niro) and Tommy DeVito (Joe Pesci), Henry enjoys the perks of the criminal life—money, power, and respect. But as the violence escalates and the paranoia sets in, Henry discovers that the very lifestyle he coveted comes with a devastating price.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Martin Scorsese's magnum opus redefined the crime genre and remains one of the most influential films in cinema history. Goodfellas isn't just a movie about gangsters—it's a masterclass in storytelling, character development, and cinematic technique that pulls you into its world and never lets go.

The film's opening line—"As far back as I can remember, I always wanted to be a gangster"—sets the tone for a story that's both a glamorization and condemnation of the mob lifestyle. Ray Liotta's magnetic performance as Henry Hill perfectly captures the allure and eventual horror of living outside society's rules.

Joe Pesci's Oscar-winning performance as the volatile Tommy DeVito is nothing short of legendary. The famous "Funny how?" scene remains one of cinema's most tense and brilliantly acted moments. Robert De Niro brings quiet menace to Jimmy Conway, while Lorraine Bracco's Karen provides the crucial perspective of someone drawn into this world against her better judgment.

Scorsese's direction is virtuosic—from the groundbreaking long take through the Copacabana to the frantic cocaine-fueled final act. The use of popular music to underscore emotional beats creates an immersive experience that makes you feel like you're living through these moments alongside the characters.

Final verdict: A perfect film in every sense. Goodfellas represents the pinnacle of American cinema and continues to influence filmmakers decades after its release.

The Making of Goodfellas

Based on Nicholas Pileggi's book "Wiseguy," Goodfellas was a passion project for Martin Scorsese, who saw in Henry Hill's story the perfect vehicle to explore themes of loyalty, betrayal, and the American Dream corrupted. Scorsese and Pileggi collaborated closely on the screenplay, with the director bringing his own childhood memories of mobsters in Little Italy to the project.

The famous Copacabana long take—where Henry takes Karen through the kitchen and into the nightclub—required meticulous planning and multiple takes to perfect. This single continuous shot, lasting nearly three minutes, immediately establishes Henry's power and status while immersing the audience in his glamorous world.

Joe Pesci's iconic "Funny how?" scene was largely improvised based on a real incident from his youth. Pesci told Scorsese about a time he complimented a mobster on being funny, only to have the man turn dangerously serious. The other actors' reactions in the scene are genuine—they weren't expecting Pesci's intensity.

Scorsese's use of music was revolutionary. Instead of commissioning an original score, he curated a soundtrack of period-appropriate songs that comment on the action. From the opening chords of "Rags to Riches" to the chilling use of "Layla" during the discovery of the murdered associates, every musical choice serves the narrative.
