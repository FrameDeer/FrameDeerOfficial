Bullet Train (2022)

Genre: Action, Comedy, Thriller | Runtime: 126 min | Director: David Leitch

Ladybug is an unlucky assassin determined to do his job peacefully after one too many gigs has gone off the rails. Fate, however, may have other plans as his latest mission puts him on a collision course with lethal adversaries from around the globe—all with connected yet conflicting objectives—on the world's fastest train. From Tokyo to Kyoto, the assassins' paths cross and their missions intertwine in a chaotic, violent, and darkly comedic game of survival. As the train speeds toward its final destination, each character must confront their past and fight for their future in this stylish, high-octane thrill ride.

FrameDeer Thoughts

David Leitch's stylish action-comedy delivers a wildly entertaining ride that combines brutal fight choreography with sharp wit and unexpected emotional depth. Bullet Train embraces its absurd premise with infectious energy, creating a self-aware, hyper-stylized world where the laws of physics and probability are regularly defied in service of pure entertainment. The film feels like a live-action anime brought to life, complete with exaggerated characters, over-the-top action, and a vibrant color palette.

The film's greatest strength is its charismatic ensemble cast and their chemistry. Brad Pitt anchors the madness with a wonderfully understated performance as Ladybug, playing the world's unluckiest assassin with a zen-like acceptance of chaos. His deadpan delivery and physical comedy provide the perfect counterpoint to the surrounding mayhem. The supporting assassins—each with their own distinctive personality and fighting style—create a delightful mosaic of conflicting motivations and dark humor.

The action sequences are choreographed with Leitch's signature flair, blending practical stunts with creative camerawork and editing. Each fight feels distinct and character-driven, from the brutal efficiency of Lemon and Tangerine to the more chaotic style of Ladybug. The confined spaces of the train create a unique challenge that the filmmakers use to their advantage, forcing creative solutions and intimate confrontations that build tension throughout the journey.

What sets Bullet Train apart from typical action fare is its surprising emotional core and thematic depth. Beneath the surface-level chaos lies a meditation on luck, fate, and the interconnectedness of seemingly random events. The film's recurring motif of Thomas the Tank Engine becomes both a running gag and a surprisingly effective metaphor for character development and narrative structure.

The production design and cinematography create a visually stunning experience that never feels repetitive despite the single location. Each train car has its own distinct personality and color scheme, helping to orient viewers while maintaining visual interest. The integration of Japanese culture and aesthetics adds another layer of style without veering into cultural appropriation.

Final verdict: A wildly entertaining action-comedy that doesn't take itself too seriously while delivering expertly crafted set pieces and memorable characters. While it may not be deep cinema, Bullet Train succeeds magnificently at being exactly what it sets out to be—a fun, stylish, and thoroughly enjoyable ride.

The Making of Bullet Train

David Leitch's adaptation of Kōtarō Isaka's novel "Maria Beetle" faced the unique challenge of translating a Japanese-set story with an entirely Japanese cast into an international production while maintaining the spirit of the source material. Leitch and screenwriter Zak Olkewicz made the deliberate choice to keep the Japanese setting while incorporating a diverse international cast of assassins, creating a global feel that served the story's themes of interconnectedness.

Brad Pitt's involvement was crucial to the film's tone and style. The actor embraced the physical comedy and action requirements, performing many of his own stunts despite being in his late 50s during production. Pitt's collaboration with Leitch—reuniting after their work on "Once Upon a Time in Hollywood"—created a comfortable working environment that allowed for improvisation and creative experimentation. His character's unlucky but zen-like persona became the emotional anchor around which the chaotic narrative revolves.

The production design team faced the significant challenge of recreating a Japanese bullet train on soundstages in Los Angeles. They built multiple train cars that could be reconfigured for different scenes, with careful attention to authentic Japanese design elements. The cars were mounted on gimbals to simulate the train's movement, and visual effects were used to create the passing scenery visible through the windows.

The fight choreography was carefully designed to reflect each character's personality and background. Ladybug's fighting style emphasizes improvisation and using whatever objects are at hand, while Lemon and Tangerine employ more disciplined, coordinated techniques. The confined spaces of the train cars required creative solutions for fight scenes, with the choreography team working closely with the production designers to ensure the action felt dynamic while remaining believable within the limited space.

Despite filming during the COVID-19 pandemic, the production maintained its ambitious schedule through strict safety protocols. The ensemble cast's chemistry was particularly impressive given the challenges of pandemic-era filming, with many actors noting the collaborative atmosphere that Leitch fostered on set.
