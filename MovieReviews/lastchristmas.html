Last Christmas (2019)

Genre: Romance, Comedy, Drama | Runtime: 103 min | Director: Paul Feig

Kate (Emilia Clarke) is a frustrated aspiring singer who works as an elf in a year-round Christmas shop in London. Still recovering from a serious illness and making questionable life choices, she's estranged from her family and sleeping on friends' couches. Her life takes an unexpected turn when she meets Tom (Henry Golding), a charming, optimistic man who seems too good to be true. As Tom helps Kate rediscover her joy and reconnect with her Yugoslavian immigrant family, their magical winter romance blossoms against the backdrop of a beautifully decorated London. However, Tom remains mysterious—appearing and disappearing unexpectedly—leading to a surprising revelation that changes everything Kate thought she knew about love, second chances, and the true meaning of Christmas.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Paul Feig's holiday romance attempts to blend classic Christmas movie tropes with a modern sensibility and a surprising twist, all set to the music of George Michael and Wham! While the film doesn't fully succeed in balancing its competing tones and narrative ambitions, it delivers enough charm, London atmosphere, and genuine emotion to make for a pleasant, if flawed, seasonal viewing experience.

Emilia Clarke shines in her first major romantic lead role following Game of Thrones, bringing her signature warmth and vulnerability to the character of Kate. Her comedic timing is sharp, and she effectively portrays Kate's journey from self-destructive cynicism to hopeful redemption. Henry Golding continues his charming leading man streak with a performance that balances mystery and genuine likability, though the script doesn't always give him enough material to work with beyond being the perfect romantic interest.

The film's greatest strength lies in its supporting cast and London setting. Emma Thompson (who also co-wrote the screenplay) delivers a scene-stealing performance as Kate's worried, eccentric mother, bringing both humor and emotional depth to her role as a Yugoslavian immigrant still processing her difficult past. Michelle Yeoh is delightful as Kate's no-nonsense boss "Santa," providing wise counsel and dry wit. London itself becomes a character, with the city's Christmas decorations and landmarks creating a magical backdrop that enhances the romantic atmosphere.

Where the film struggles is in its tonal inconsistency and the handling of its central twist. The shift from light romantic comedy to more serious drama feels somewhat jarring, and the big revelation—while emotionally impactful—may divide audiences. The George Michael/Wham! soundtrack is used extensively, sometimes to great effect in capturing the festive spirit, but at other times feeling overly reliant on the pre-existing emotional resonance of the songs rather than earning those moments through the storytelling.

Final verdict: A flawed but ultimately warm-hearted Christmas film that works better as a seasonal mood piece than as a tightly constructed narrative. While it may not become a timeless holiday classic, Last Christmas offers enough genuine emotion, strong performances, and festive cheer to satisfy viewers looking for undemanding seasonal entertainment with a surprising emotional punch.

The Making of Last Christmas

The film originated from Emma Thompson's idea to create a Christmas movie set in London that would incorporate the music of George Michael and Wham! Thompson, a longtime fan of Michael's music, worked on the screenplay with playwright Bryony Lavery. The project gained momentum when Paul Feig signed on to direct, attracted by the opportunity to make a Christmas film and work with Thompson. The production received full cooperation from George Michael's estate, allowing them to use his music extensively throughout the film.

Casting Emilia Clarke was a deliberate choice to showcase her comedic talents beyond her famous Game of Thrones role. Clarke embraced the opportunity to play a more contemporary, flawed character and worked closely with the director to develop Kate's journey from self-destruction to redemption. Henry Golding continued his rapid rise following Crazy Rich Asians, bringing his natural charm to the mysterious Tom. The casting of Michelle Yeoh provided another connection to Golding's breakthrough film and added star power to the supporting cast.

The production filmed entirely in London during the actual Christmas season of 2018-2019, allowing them to capture authentic holiday atmosphere and decorations. Key locations included Covent Garden, where the Christmas shop was recreated, and various iconic London landmarks dressed in their Christmas finest. The timing also meant the cast and crew experienced genuine winter weather, adding to the film's authentic seasonal feel.

One of the film's most discussed elements—the twist ending—was carefully guarded during production. The cast and crew took measures to prevent spoilers, understanding that the emotional impact relied on audience surprise. This narrative choice, while divisive, demonstrated the filmmakers' willingness to take risks with the conventional Christmas movie formula and address more serious themes alongside the romantic comedy elements.
