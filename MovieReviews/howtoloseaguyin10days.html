How to Lose a Guy in 10 Days (2003)

Genre: Romance, Comedy | Runtime: 116 min | Director: Donald Petrie

Andie Anderson (Kate Hudson), a "How To" columnist for Composure magazine, is assigned to write an article on how to get a man to leave you in 10 days, based on all the mistakes women make in relationships. Simultaneously, advertising executive Benjamin Barry (Matthew McConaughey) bets his colleagues that he can make any woman fall in love with him in 10 days to win a lucrative diamond account. When their paths cross at a party, Andie targets Ben as her unsuspecting guinea pig, while Ben sees Andie as the perfect woman to win his bet. What follows is a hilarious battle of wills as Andie employs every relationship faux pas imaginable to drive Ben away, while Ben desperately tries to win her heart, leading to increasingly absurd situations and unexpected real feelings that complicate both their agendas.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Donald Petrie directs a quintessential early-2000s romantic comedy that has aged into a beloved cult classic despite mixed initial reviews. How to Lose a Guy in 10 Days succeeds primarily due to the electric chemistry between its two leads and the clever premise that allows for both hilarious comedy and genuine romantic tension.

The film's strength lies in its perfectly matched leads and their commitment to the absurd premise. Kate Hudson shines as Andie, balancing her character's professional ambition with the vulnerability she begins to feel as real emotions complicate her assignment. Her portrayal of increasingly outrageous relationship behaviors—from naming his private parts to creating a "love fern"—is both cringe-inducingly funny and surprisingly endearing.

Matthew McConaughey delivers one of his most charming romantic comedy performances as Ben, perfectly capturing the character's transformation from smooth ad executive to genuinely smitten boyfriend. His ability to maintain his cool while dealing with Andie's escalating antics provides much of the film's humor, while his growing authentic affection for her gives the romance its emotional weight.

The supporting cast adds considerable flavor to the film, with Bebe Neuwirth stealing scenes as Andie's tough-but-fair editor and Robert Klein providing solid support as Ben's advertising mentor. The New York setting feels glamorous and romantic, with stylish production design that captures the early 2000s aesthetic while maintaining timeless appeal.

While the film follows familiar romantic comedy tropes, it executes them with such energy and charm that it feels fresh and entertaining. The third-act conflict, when both characters discover they've been deceived, delivers genuine emotional stakes that make the eventual reconciliation feel earned rather than contrived.

Final verdict: A delightful romantic comedy that represents the peak of early-2000s rom-com filmmaking. While not groundbreaking in its storytelling, it delivers exactly what fans of the genre want: laughs, chemistry, and a satisfying romantic payoff, all wrapped in a stylish package with two stars at the top of their game.

The Making of How to Lose a Guy in 10 Days

How to Lose a Guy in 10 Days was based on Michelle Alexander and Jeannie Long's popular 2001 book of the same name, which humorously documented relationship mistakes women make. The film adaptation expanded the concept by adding the male perspective and the bet storyline, creating the perfect setup for romantic comedy conflict.

The casting of Kate Hudson and Matthew McConaughey proved to be inspired, with their natural chemistry evident from their first screen test. Hudson was coming off her Oscar-nominated role in Almost Famous and proved her comedic chops, while McConaughey was establishing himself as a romantic lead following successes like The Wedding Planner. Their off-screen friendship translated into believable on-screen romance.

The production faced the challenge of making Andie's increasingly outrageous behavior both funny and believable. Director Donald Petrie worked with Hudson to find the right balance between comedy and character authenticity. The famous "love fern" scene, where Andie dramatically mourns a dying plant, became one of the film's most memorable moments and spawned countless references in popular culture.

The New York setting was crucial to establishing the film's glamorous, cosmopolitan atmosphere. While some scenes were shot in New Orleans for tax reasons, extensive New York location shooting helped capture the energy of the city that serves as a backdrop for Andie and Ben's romance. The stylish production design and costumes perfectly captured early-2000s fashion while maintaining timeless appeal.

The film's success helped cement the "bet" trope as a romantic comedy staple and demonstrated the commercial viability of well-executed romantic comedies in the early 2000s. Its enduring popularity on streaming platforms and cable television has introduced it to new generations of viewers, ensuring its status as a rom-com classic.
