Begin Again (2013)

Genre: Drama, Music, Romance | Runtime: 104 min | Director: John Carney

A disgraced music executive and a young singer-songwriter, both at personal and professional crossroads, form an unlikely partnership to create music in the raw, unfiltered streets of New York City. Dan Mulligan (Mark Ruffalo), recently fired from the record label he co-founded, discovers Gretta James (Keira Knightley) performing in a small club and sees in her the authentic talent that's missing from today's music industry. Together, they embark on a unique project: recording an album outdoors using various locations throughout New York as their studio, finding redemption and new beginnings through their shared love of music.

FrameDeer Thoughts

John Carney creates a charming and heartfelt musical drama that celebrates the transformative power of music and human connection. Begin Again is a love letter to New York City, authentic artistry, and second chances that resonates with anyone who's ever needed to start over.

The film's strength lies in its authentic portrayal of the music industry and the creative process. Unlike many music-focused films, Begin Again captures the raw, unpolished magic of music creation—from the initial spark of an idea to the collaborative energy of bringing a song to life in unexpected urban spaces.

Mark Ruffalo delivers a wonderfully nuanced performance as Dan Mulligan, a man drowning in professional failure and personal regret. His portrayal of a music executive who's lost his passion but still has an ear for genuine talent is both heartbreaking and inspiring. The chemistry between Ruffalo and Knightley forms the emotional core of the film.

Keira Knightley surprises with her musical abilities, performing her own vocals and bringing a natural, unassuming quality to Gretta. Her character's journey from heartbroken songwriter to confident artist feels authentic and earned. Adam Levine makes a solid acting debut as Dave, Gretta's rising star boyfriend who represents the commercial side of the music industry.

The original songs by Gregg Alexander and others are genuinely memorable, particularly "Lost Stars" which serves as the film's emotional centerpiece. The innovative recording sessions throughout New York City create a magical, almost fairytale-like atmosphere that elevates the film beyond typical romantic drama territory.

Final verdict: A warm, uplifting film about redemption, creativity, and the connections that help us find our way back to ourselves. Begin Again reminds us that sometimes the most beautiful music comes from the most broken places.

The Making of Begin Again

Begin Again was written and directed by John Carney, following his success with the indie musical Once. The film was originally titled "Can a Song Save Your Life?" before being changed to Begin Again for its wider release. Carney drew inspiration from his own experiences in the music industry and wanted to explore themes of artistic integrity versus commercial success.

The film's unique approach to recording scenes was carefully planned to capture authentic musical moments. All the outdoor recording sessions were filmed on location in New York City, using real city sounds and ambient noise to create the film's distinctive sonic landscape. The "split-cable" scene where Dan imagines the orchestration for Gretta's song was achieved through careful editing and practical effects.

Keira Knightley's casting was initially met with skepticism due to her lack of musical background, but she dedicated herself to learning guitar and worked with vocal coaches to perform her own songs. Her natural singing voice surprised both critics and audiences, bringing an authentic quality to her performance as a budding songwriter.

Mark Ruffalo brought his own musical background to the role, having been in a band during his younger years. His chemistry with Knightley developed naturally through rehearsals and the shared experience of creating music together on screen. Adam Levine, making his acting debut, drew from his own experiences as a musician to inform his portrayal of Dave Kohl.

The soundtrack was curated by Gregg Alexander of New Radicals, who came out of musical retirement to work on the film. The song "Lost Stars" became the film's signature track, performed by both Knightley and Levine in different styles that reflect their characters' contrasting approaches to music.
