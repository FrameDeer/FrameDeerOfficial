Martin Scorsese

Date of Birth: November 17, 1942

Location of Birth: Queens, New York City, USA

Height: 5'4" (163 cm)

Martin Scorsese is an Academy Award-winning filmmaker and one of the most influential directors in cinema history. Known for his gritty, character-driven films exploring themes of crime, guilt, and redemption, he has created some of the most iconic American films of the past five decades while also being a passionate film preservationist.

Early Career

Martin Scorsese grew up in New York's Little Italy and initially considered becoming a priest before discovering his passion for film. He attended New York University's Tisch School of the Arts, where he made his first short films. His feature debut Who's That Knocking at My Door (1967) caught the attention of influential film critic Roger Ebert. He worked as an editor on Woodstock (1970) before directing Boxcar Bertha (1972) for Roger Corman. His breakthrough came with Mean Streets (1973), which established his signature style and began his long collaboration with Robert De Niro, launching him as a major new voice in American cinema.

Signature Style & Themes

New York Stories

Deep exploration of New York City's underworld and urban landscapes, particularly Little Italy and Manhattan.

Catholic Guilt

Recurring themes of sin, redemption, and Catholic morality in characters' struggles with conscience.

Tracking Shots

Masterful use of long, fluid tracking shots to create immersive experiences and establish environments.

Rock Music

Innovative use of popular music and rock'n'roll to enhance narrative and emotional impact.

Violence & Morality

Exploration of masculine violence, organized crime, and the moral consequences of criminal life.

Character Studies

Deep psychological portraits of complex, often morally ambiguous characters on society's margins.
